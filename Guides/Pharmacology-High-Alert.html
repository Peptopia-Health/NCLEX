⚠️ High-Alert Medication Principles

⚡ High-alert medications bear a heightened risk of significant patient harm when used in error. They may NOT be the most common error sources, but the consequences are devastating.

ISMP High-Alert Categories

  • Anticoagulants — heparin, warfarin, enoxaparin, DOACs
  • Insulins — all formulations, especially concentrated (U-500)
  • Opioids — IV, transdermal, and oral concentrated forms
  • Chemotherapy agents — IV and oral antineoplastics
  • Electrolyte concentrates — IV potassium, hypertonic saline, IV magnesium
  • Neuromuscular blocking agents — succinylcholine, vecuronium
  • Sedation agents — propofol, midazolam, ketamine
  • Cardioplegic solutions and parenteral nutrition

Independent Double-Check Process

Two qualified clinicians independently verify high-alert medications before administration:

  • ✅ Right drug, dose, route, rate, patient, concentration
  • ✅ Pump settings verified separately (not reading over each other's shoulder)
  • ✅ Required for: insulin drips, heparin drips, PCA pumps, chemotherapy, epidurals
  • ❌ Does NOT replace barcode scanning — use BOTH systems

🩸 Heparin Safety

  • Never abbreviate "U" for units — write out
  • aPTT drawn 6 hrs after dose change
  • HIT: platelet count drops >50% → stop ALL heparin
  • SQ injection: abdomen, do NOT aspirate, do NOT rub
  • Protamine: 1 mg per 100 units heparin

💊 Warfarin Safety

  • INR checked frequently until stable, then monthly
  • Vitamin K foods: consistent intake, don't eliminate
  • Avoid NSAIDs (↑ bleeding risk)
  • Takes 3–5 days for full effect (bridge with heparin)
  • Teratogenic — Category X in pregnancy
🚨 Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia (HIT): Platelets drop >50% within 5–10 days of heparin. Paradoxically causes thrombosis, not bleeding. Stop ALL heparin (including flushes). Switch to argatroban or bivalirudin.

Thrombolytics (Fibrinolytics)

  • Alteplase (tPA): MI within 12 hrs, stroke within 3–4.5 hrs, PE
  • Risk: hemorrhage — monitor neuro checks, bleeding sites
  • Contraindications: active bleeding, recent surgery (14 days), uncontrolled HTN, history hemorrhagic stroke
  • No IM injections, arterial punctures, or invasive procedures for 24 hrs
  • Aminocaproic acid (Amicar) reverses if needed
🩸 Bleeding precautions for ALL anticoagulants: Soft toothbrush, electric razor, avoid contact sports, report dark stool/hematuria/unusual bruising, wear medical alert bracelet.

⚡ Only Regular insulin can be given IV. All others are SQ only. Regular insulin is also the ONLY insulin that is clear AND can be mixed with NPH.

Mixing Rules

  • "Clear before Cloudy" — draw Regular first, then NPH
  • NEVER mix glargine or detemir with anything
  • Gently roll NPH vial — never shake
  • Once mixed, administer immediately

Storage & Admin

  • Unopened: refrigerate. In-use: room temp 28 days
  • Rotate injection sites (abdomen fastest absorption)
  • Use insulin syringes only — never TB syringes
  • Pen needles: single use, never share pens

Hypoglycemia Management — Rule of 15

  • BG < 70: 15g fast-acting carbs → recheck in 15 min → repeat if still < 70
  • Conscious: 4 oz juice, glucose tablets, hard candy
  • Unconscious: Glucagon IM/SQ or Dextrose 50% IV push
  • On acarbose/miglitol: Use pure glucose only (these block sugar digestion)
  • Symptoms: tremor, diaphoresis, tachycardia, confusion, irritability
📋 DKA vs HHS: DKA = Type 1, rapid onset, Kussmaul breathing, fruity breath, BG 300–800. HHS = Type 2, gradual onset, severe dehydration, BG >600 (often >1000). Both need IV fluids + Regular insulin drip. Monitor K⁺ closely — insulin drives K⁺ into cells.

🚨 Respiratory Depression = Priority #1: RR < 12 → hold dose & notify provider. RR < 8 or unresponsive → Naloxone (Narcan) 0.4–2 mg IV, may repeat q2–3 min. Monitor 1–2 hrs post-naloxone (shorter half-life than most opioids).

PCA Pump Safety

  • Only the patient presses the button — NEVER family/visitors ("PCA by proxy" = unsafe)
  • Lockout interval prevents overdose (typically 6–10 min)
  • Independent double-check at initiation and rate changes
  • Monitor: sedation level, RR, O₂ sat, pain score
  • Sedation scale (Pasero): S = sleep/easy to arouse → 4 = unarousable → STOP PCA

Non-Opioid Adjuncts

  • Ketorolac (Toradol): NSAID, max 5 days IV/IM, GI bleeding risk
  • Gabapentin/Pregabalin: neuropathic pain, dizziness/drowsiness
  • Acetaminophen: max 4g/day (2g if liver disease), no anti-inflammatory effect
  • Lidocaine patch: topical for post-herpetic neuralgia
🚫 Meperidine (Demerol): Avoid in elderly and renal impairment. Metabolite normeperidine accumulates → seizures, CNS excitation. Not recommended for chronic pain.

❤️ Cardiac High-Alert Drugs

Digoxin (Lanoxin)

  • Therapeutic range: 0.5–2.0 ng/mL
  • Check apical HR × 60 sec — hold if < 60 BPM (adult) or < 100 (infant)
  • Hypokalemia ↑ toxicity risk — maintain K⁺ 3.5–5.0
  • Toxicity signs: N/V, anorexia, visual changes (halos), bradycardia, dysrhythmias
  • Antidote: Digoxin immune Fab (Digibind)
  • Avoid with amiodarone, verapamil (↑ dig levels)

Dopamine (dose-dependent)

  • Low (1–5 mcg/kg/min): renal vasodilation
  • Moderate (5–10): ↑ cardiac output (β₁)
  • High (10–20): vasoconstriction (α₁)
  • Extravasation → tissue necrosis; antidote: phentolamine

Dobutamine

  • Positive inotrope (↑ contractility)
  • Heart failure with low cardiac output
  • May ↓ BP (vasodilation)
  • NOT for hypovolemic shock (correct volume first)

Amiodarone (Cordarone)

  • Class III antiarrhythmic — V-tach, V-fib, A-fib
  • Pulmonary toxicity: monitor CXR, PFTs, cough/dyspnea
  • Thyroid dysfunction: contains iodine — hypo or hyperthyroidism
  • Hepatotoxicity: monitor LFTs
  • Photosensitivity: blue-gray skin discoloration
  • Long half-life (40–55 days) — effects persist weeks after stopping
  • IV: use filter, give via central line preferred, monitor BP closely

Nitroglycerin & Nitroprusside

  • Nitroglycerin: SL q5min × 3, headache expected, no PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil)
  • IV NTG: glass bottles, non-PVC tubing (drug absorbs into plastic)
  • Nitroprusside: powerful vasodilator, cyanide toxicity risk with prolonged use
  • Protect nitroprusside from light (wrap in foil), monitor thiocyanate levels
  • Both cause hypotension — monitor BP q5min during titration
⚡ Adenosine: Given by rapid IV push (1–2 sec) followed by immediate NS flush. Causes brief asystole (warn patient). Half-life 6 seconds. Used for SVT only.

🧪 Chemotherapy Safety

Safe Handling & Administration

  • PPE: Double gloves (chemo-rated), gown, eye protection, closed-system transfer devices
  • Prepare in biological safety cabinet (BSC)
  • Spill kit immediately available, clean with detergent (not alcohol)
  • Body fluids hazardous for 48 hrs — double-flush toilet, wear gloves for linen
  • Pregnant nurses should NOT handle chemotherapy
  • Oral chemo: do not crush, break, or chew — handle with gloves

Vesicants

  • Doxorubicin (red): cardiotoxicity, max lifetime dose
  • Vincristine: neurotoxicity — IV ONLY, never intrathecal
  • Extravasation → severe tissue necrosis
  • Protocol: STOP infusion, aspirate, apply cold (most) or heat (vinca alkaloids)
  • Antidote: dexrazoxane (doxorubicin)

Nadir & Neutropenia

  • Nadir: lowest WBC/platelet count, typically 7–14 days post-chemo
  • Neutropenic precautions when ANC < 500
  • No fresh flowers, raw fruits/vegetables (neutropenic diet)
  • Avoid rectal temps, suppositories, IM injections
  • Filgrastim (Neupogen): boosts WBC, bone pain expected
🚨 Tumor Lysis Syndrome: Rapid cell death releases contents → ↑K⁺, ↑phosphate, ↑uric acid, ↓Ca²⁺. Prevent with allopurinol/rasburicase + aggressive IV hydration. Can cause fatal cardiac arrhythmias from hyperkalemia.
🔴 Doxorubicin cardiotoxicity: Cumulative lifetime dose limit (450–550 mg/m²). Monitor ejection fraction (ECHO/MUGA). Red-orange urine is expected (not blood).

🚨 NEVER IV push potassium. Can cause fatal cardiac arrest. Always dilute and infuse on a pump with cardiac monitoring. Max concentration: 40 mEq/L peripheral, 80 mEq/L central.

Magnesium Sulfate Safety

  • Before each dose: check DTRs (absent = toxicity), RR ≥ 12, UO ≥ 30 mL/hr
  • Therapeutic level: 4–7 mEq/L (preeclampsia), 1.5–2.5 mEq/L (normal)
  • Toxicity progression: Loss of DTRs → respiratory depression → cardiac arrest
  • Antidote: Calcium gluconate at bedside
  • Used for: preeclampsia/eclampsia, torsades de pointes, severe hypomagnesemia

Hypertonic Saline (3% NaCl)

  • Used for severe hyponatremia (Na⁺ < 120 with symptoms)
  • Administer via central line with infusion pump
  • Monitor Na⁺ q2h — raise no faster than 8–12 mEq/24 hrs
  • Risk of osmotic demyelination syndrome (central pontine myelinolysis) if corrected too fast
  • Assess for fluid overload: crackles, JVD, I&O, daily weights
💉 Blood product safety: Two-nurse verification at bedside. Use Y-tubing with NS only (no LR/dextrose). Stay with patient first 15 min. If reaction → STOP, NS wide open, send blood bag to lab.

🧠 High-Yield Mnemonics
  • "Warfarin = Wed-INR-sday" — Warfarin monitored by INR (extrinsic pathway → PT/INR)
  • "HIT = Heparin Is Thrombotic" — HIT causes clotting, not bleeding
  • "Clear before Cloudy" — Draw Regular (clear) before NPH (cloudy)
  • "Never push K" — IV potassium = always diluted, always on a pump
  • "Dig and Check" — Digoxin → check level AND check HR before giving
  • "Low and Slow for Mag" — Check DTRs, RR, UO → calcium gluconate at bedside
  • "Red Man from Vanc" — Infuse slowly (> 60 min) to prevent Red Man Syndrome
  • "Amino = Ears + Kidneys" — Aminoglycosides → ototoxicity + nephrotoxicity
✅ NCLEX Priority: High-alert questions test SAFETY. Think: What could KILL the patient? Anticoagulant bleeding, insulin hypoglycemia, opioid respiratory depression, K⁺ cardiac arrest, chemo extravasation — these are the situations you intervene FIRST.
