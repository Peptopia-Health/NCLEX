1 ⚗️ Endocrine — DKA vs. HHS
Core Concept: DKA affects Type 1 diabetics with absolute insulin deficiency, rapid onset, and ketosis. HHS affects Type 2 diabetics with profound dehydration, gradual onset, and no ketosis. Both are life-threatening hyperglycemic emergencies.

💊 Pharmacological Interventions

IV Fluid Protocol

  • 0.9% Normal Saline (isotonic) — Initiate first to restore circulating volume
  • Switch to D5W ½NS (5% Dextrose with 0.45% NS) when glucose reaches ~250 mg/dL to prevent hypoglycemia

Insulin & Electrolyte Management

  • Regular Insulin (IV) — The only insulin that can be administered intravenously. Given as continuous infusion.
  • Potassium Chloride (KCl) — Add to IV fluids once urine output is adequate. Insulin drives K+ back into cells, causing severe hypokalemia.
NCLEX Key: Always prime the IV tubing with Regular Insulin solution before initiating the drip — insulin adheres to plastic tubing.

🟦 ADPIE Case Study: The DKA Patient

AAssessment: Kussmaul respirations, fruity breath odor, lethargy, poor skin turgor, tachycardia.
DxDiagnosis: Deficient fluid volume related to osmotic diuresis; Risk for electrolyte imbalance.
PPlanning: Restore fluid volume, correct acidosis, normalize blood glucose levels slowly.
IImplementation: Establish two large-bore IVs. Administer bolus of 0.9% NS. Prime IV tubing with Regular Insulin before initiating drip. Monitor blood glucose hourly. Monitor telemetry for peaked T-waves (hyperkalemia) or prominent U-waves (hypokalemia).
EEvaluation: Resolution of acidosis (HCO3 > 18, pH > 7.30), patient alert and oriented, blood glucose stable 150–200 mg/dL.
2 🦠 Infectious Disease — Sepsis & Septic Shock
Core Concept: Sepsis is life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection. Septic shock is a subset with profound circulatory, cellular, and metabolic abnormalities.

🔬 Diagnostic Labs

ProcalcitoninBacterial infection biomarkerElevated = bacterial etiology

💊 Pharmacological Interventions

Sepsis 1-Hour Bundle

TIME-CRITICAL
  • Broad-Spectrum Antibiotics (e.g., Piperacillin/Tazobactam, Vancomycin) — Administer within the first hour of recognition
  • 30 mL/kg crystalloid fluids for hypotension or lactate ≥ 4 mmol/L

Vasopressors & Adjuncts

  • Norepinephrine — First-line vasopressor if hypotension persists after adequate fluid resuscitation
  • Hydrocortisone — Consider for refractory shock unresponsive to fluids and vasopressors
Critical Alert: Blood cultures must be drawn BEFORE antibiotic administration. However, antibiotic delivery must not be delayed — if cultures are logistically difficult, administer antibiotics first. Time kills in sepsis.

🟦 ADPIE Case Study: The Septic Patient

AAssessment: Temp > 38.3°C or < 36.0°C, HR > 90, RR > 20, altered mental status, MAP < 65 mmHg.
DxDiagnosis: Impaired tissue perfusion; Infection.
PPlanning: Administer 1-hour bundle interventions. Restore MAP to ≥ 65 mmHg.
IImplementation: Draw lactic acid and blood cultures. Administer 30 mL/kg crystalloid for hypotension or lactate ≥ 4. Administer broad-spectrum antibiotics.
EEvaluation: Lactate clearance, normalization of vital signs, improved urine output (> 0.5 mL/kg/hr).
3 🧒 Peds Heme/Onc — Sickle Cell Crisis
Core Concept: Vaso-occlusive crisis is the most common and painful complication of sickle cell anemia. Sickled erythrocytes obstruct microcirculation, causing tissue ischemia, pain, and cellular infarction.

🔬 Diagnostic Labs

LabExpected FindingClinical Significance
Hemoglobin7–10 g/dL baselineAcute drops → aplastic or splenic sequestration crisis
Reticulocyte CountElevatedCompensatory bone marrow production from rapid RBC destruction
BilirubinElevatedRBC hemolysis byproduct

💊 Pharmacological Interventions

Pain Management

  • Morphine or Hydromorphone via PCA pump — administer on a scheduled, around-the-clock basis (NOT PRN)
  • Avoid Meperidine (Demerol) — high risk of normeperidine-induced seizures

Disease Modifiers & Hydration

  • Hydroxyurea — Increases fetal hemoglobin (HbF) production, reducing frequency of sickling crises
  • 0.45% Normal Saline — Aggressive IV hydration to reduce blood viscosity and prevent further sickling
Critical Nursing Actions: Apply WARM compresses to painful joints. NEVER use cold — cold promotes vasoconstriction and sickling. Provide supplemental O2 only if hypoxic (SpO2 < 92%).

🟦 ADPIE Case Study: Pediatric Sickle Cell Patient

AAssessment: Severe, localized pain (extremities, back, abdomen), joint swelling (dactylitis in infants), fever.
DxDiagnosis: Acute pain related to tissue ischemia; Deficient fluid volume.
PPlanning: Achieve acceptable pain level (patient-defined), optimize hydration, prevent hypoxemia.
IImplementation: Administer high-flow IV fluids. Administer scheduled Morphine around the clock. Apply warm compresses. Provide O2 only if SpO2 < 92%.
EEvaluation: Patient reports pain is manageable, O2 saturation maintained on room air, adequate and clear urine output.
4 ❤️ Cardiovascular — Heart Failure
Core Concept: Left-sided HF affects pulmonary circulation — fluid backs up into lungs (pulmonary edema). Right-sided HF (Cor Pulmonale) affects systemic circulation — fluid backs up into the venous system (peripheral edema, JVD, hepatomegaly).

💊 Pharmacological Interventions

Loop Diuretics

  • Furosemide or Bumetanide — Rapidly decrease preload by promoting Na+ and water excretion
  • Nursing priority: Assess serum K+ before administration — high risk of hypokalemia
  • Administer IV push over 1–2 minutes to prevent ototoxicity

ACE Inhibitors

  • Lisinopril or Enalapril — Block RAAS, promoting vasodilation and decreasing afterload
  • Monitor for: Dry, hacking cough or life-threatening angioedema

Beta-Blockers & Cardiac Glycosides

  • Metoprolol / Carvedilol — Decrease HR and myocardial O2 demand. Hold if HR < 60 or SBP < 100.
  • Digoxin — Positive inotrope, negative chronotrope. Check apical pulse × 1 full minute before giving. Toxicity worsened by hypokalemia (visual disturbances, N/V, bradycardia).

🟦 ADPIE Case Study: Acute Decompensated Heart Failure

AAssessment: Severe dyspnea at rest, orthopnea, PND, bilateral crackles, pink frothy sputum, RR 28.
DxDiagnosis: Impaired gas exchange r/t alveolar fluid accumulation; Decreased cardiac output r/t altered contractility.
PPlanning: Improve oxygenation, rapidly reduce preload, decrease myocardial workload.
IImplementation: Elevate HOB to high-Fowler's. O2 to maintain SpO2 > 94%. IV Furosemide over 1–2 min. Insert Foley for strict I&O. Continuous telemetry for ventricular dysrhythmias.
EEvaluation: Unlabored RR 16, clear lung sounds, SpO2 96% on room air, UO > 100 mL/hr.
5 🦴 Musculoskeletal — Ortho Emergencies
Two Critical Emergencies: Compartment Syndrome — increased pressure within a myofascial compartment compromises circulation and leads to necrosis. Fat Embolism Syndrome (FES) — fat globules from fractured long bones occlude microvasculature of lungs, brain, and skin.

💊 Pharmacological Interventions

Pain & Fluid Management

  • Morphine / Fentanyl — For severe pain. In compartment syndrome, pain is characteristically unresponsive to opioids (a primary clinical hallmark).
  • 0.9% Normal Saline — Aggressive crystalloid resuscitation for both conditions; flushes myoglobin through kidneys in rhabdomyolysis.
  • Methylprednisolone — Controversial in FES; may decrease inflammatory response to fat globules.
Critical Nursing Actions for Compartment Syndrome:
  • Keep extremity at heart level — do NOT elevate (decreases arterial perfusion pressure)
  • Do NOT apply ice (causes vasoconstriction)
  • Loosen all constrictive dressings, bivalve casts
  • Prepare patient for emergent fasciotomy

🟦 ADPIE Case Study: Post-Op Femur Fracture

AAssessment: 24 hrs post-ORIF right femur. Excruciating, burning pain in right calf rated 10/10, unrelieved by Hydromorphone. Pain worsens with passive dorsiflexion. Pulses palpable but cap refill sluggish (4 sec).
DxDiagnosis: Risk for peripheral neurovascular dysfunction r/t increased tissue pressure.
PPlanning: Prevent irreversible muscle and nerve ischemia. Facilitate immediate surgical decompression.
IImplementation: Immediately notify orthopedic surgeon. Keep extremity at heart level (do NOT elevate). Loosen constrictive dressings, bivalve casts. Do NOT apply ice. Prepare for emergent fasciotomy.
EEvaluation: Post-fasciotomy: significant pain reduction, cap refill < 2 sec, normal sensation restored distally.
