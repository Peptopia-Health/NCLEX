1 🗣️ Therapeutic Communication
Core Principle: Therapeutic communication is purposeful, goal-directed interaction that advances the patient's well-being. The nurse uses active listening, empathy, and professional boundaries — never personal opinions, false reassurance, or judgment.

✅ Therapeutic Techniques

TechniqueExampleWhy It Works
Open-ended Questions"Tell me about what you're feeling."Encourages patient to elaborate; avoids yes/no dead ends
ReflectionPatient: "I'm angry." Nurse: "You sound angry."Mirrors emotion back; validates without judging
Restating"So you're saying the voices started last week?"Clarifies and confirms understanding
SilenceSitting quietly, maintaining presenceAllows patient to collect thoughts; shows patience
Offering Self"I'll sit with you for a while."Shows availability and care without demands
Focusing"Let's talk more about the nightmares."Redirects to important topics when patient rambles
Summarizing"Today we discussed your sleep and anxiety."Organizes session; validates what was shared
Exploring"Tell me more about that experience."Deepens understanding without leading
Giving Info"This medication may cause drowsiness."Empowers patient with facts for decisions

❌ Non-Therapeutic Responses (NCLEX Traps!)

BarrierExampleWhy It Fails
False Reassurance"Everything will be fine."Dismisses real fears; patient feels unheard
Giving Advice"You should leave your partner."Removes patient autonomy; imposes nurse's values
"Why" Questions"Why did you do that?"Sounds accusatory; patient feels defensive
Changing Subject"Let's talk about something happier."Avoids patient's need; communicates discomfort
Approval/Disapproval"That was a good/bad decision."Imposes judgment; patient seeks nurse approval
Agreeing/Disagreeing"You're right" or "That's wrong."Prevents independent thinking
Belittling"Everyone goes through that."Minimizes unique suffering
NCLEX Key: When choosing the best response, eliminate false reassurance, advice-giving, and "why" questions first. The correct answer almost always acknowledges feelings and encourages the patient to talk more. Think: "What response keeps the door open?"

🤝 Phases of the Nurse-Patient Relationship

Peplau's 4 Phases

THERAPEUTIC RELATIONSHIP

1. Pre-interaction: Review chart, identify biases, plan approach. You haven't met the patient yet.

2. Orientation: Introductions, establish trust, set boundaries/goals, define roles. Most testing happens on NCLEX. Contract for safety happens here.

3. Working: Active problem-solving, therapeutic interventions, insight development. Patient may test boundaries. Transference and countertransference emerge here.

4. Termination: Summarize progress, address feelings about ending, referrals. Patient may regress — this is normal. Plan ahead by introducing termination early.

Transference vs. Countertransference
Transference = Patient projects feelings about someone onto the nurse ("You remind me of my mother who abandoned me")
Countertransference = Nurse projects personal feelings onto the patient (feeling overly protective because patient reminds nurse of a loved one)
Action: Recognize it → seek supervision → maintain professional boundaries
2 😰 Anxiety Spectrum Disorders

📊 Four Levels of Anxiety

LevelPerceptionBehaviorNursing Action
Mild (+)Alert, wide perceptual fieldMotivated, can learn, slight restlessnessUse for teaching! Optimal learning state
Moderate (++)Narrowed focus, selective inattentionDifficulty concentrating, increased HR/RR, voice changesRedirect focus, use simple clear communication

Anxiety Disorders — Comparison

DisorderKey FeaturesTreatment

Nursing Approach — OCD Patient:
✅ DO: Allow rituals when possible (reduces anxiety). Set time limits gradually. Redirect gently. Provide structured routine.
❌ DON'T: Interrupt rituals abruptly (causes panic). Argue about the irrationality. Punish or shame.

CRITICAL — Suicide Risk Peaks During Recovery! When a depressed patient suddenly seems "better" — cheerful, calm, giving away possessions — this may indicate they've decided on a plan and feel relief. Increase observation immediately.
Depression Mnemonic — SIG E CAPS:
Sleep (↑ or ↓) • Interest (loss of) • Guilt (excessive) • Energy (↓) • Concentration (↓) • Appetite (↑ or ↓) • Psychomotor (retardation/agitation) • Suicidal ideation
≥5 symptoms for ≥2 weeks = Major Depressive Episode
Nursing Approach — Manic Patient:
✅ DO: Provide calm, structured environment. Reduce stimuli (private room, low lights). Offer high-calorie finger foods. Set firm, consistent limits. Short, direct communication. Channel energy into physical activity.
❌ DON'T: Argue with grandiose beliefs. Engage in power struggles. Allow access to credit cards/phone (impulsive spending). Put in group settings during acute mania.
4 🔮 Schizophrenia & Psychotic Disorders
The Dopamine Hypothesis: Schizophrenia involves excessive dopamine activity in mesolimbic pathways (causes positive symptoms) and decreased dopamine in prefrontal cortex (causes negative symptoms). This is why antipsychotics block dopamine — and why they treat positive symptoms better than negative ones.

Positive vs. Negative Symptoms

➕ Positive Symptoms VS ➖ Negative Symptoms
Feature➕ Positive (Added to behavior)➖ Negative (Taken away from behavior)
DefinitionExcess or distortion of normal functionDiminished or absent normal function
HallucinationsAuditory most common (command, commentary). Visual, tactile, olfactory, gustatory.—
DelusionsPersecutory (#1), grandiose, referential, somatic, erotomanic, nihilistic—
Thought DisorderLoose associations, word salad, neologisms, echolalia, tangentialityPoverty of speech (alogia)
BehaviorAgitation, bizarre behavior, catatonic excitementCatatonic withdrawal, psychomotor retardation
AffectInappropriate affect (laughing at sad news)Flat affect (no emotional expression), anhedonia
Social—Avolition (no motivation), social withdrawal, poor hygiene
Treatment ResponseBetter response to typical antipsychoticsPoor response — atypicals slightly better. Most disabling long-term.
Negative Symptom Mnemonic — The 5 A's:
Affect (flat) • Alogia (poverty of speech) • Avolition (no motivation) • Anhedonia (no pleasure) • Attention deficit

ErotomanicA famous person is in love with them"The celebrity sends me love letters through TV."

Thought BroadcastingOthers can hear their thoughts"Everyone in this room can read my mind."
Thought InsertionThoughts are placed in their mind by others"The government is putting thoughts in my head."

🗣️ Types of Hallucinations

TypeDescriptionClinical Significance
AuditoryHearing voices — most common in schizophreniaCommand hallucinations ("Kill yourself") = highest risk
VisualSeeing things not thereMore common in delirium, substance use, dementia than schizophrenia
TactileFeeling things on/under skinClassic in alcohol withdrawal (DTs) and stimulant use
OlfactorySmelling odors not presentMay indicate brain tumor (temporal lobe) — requires medical workup
GustatoryTasting something in absence of stimulusOften accompanies persecutory delusions about poisoning
How to Interact with a Hallucinating Patient:
✅ DO: "I understand the voices are real to you, but I don't hear them." Present reality without arguing. Assess for command hallucinations. Distract with reality-based activities. Monitor safety.
❌ DON'T: Say "There are no voices — you're imagining things." Don't pretend you hear them too. Don't argue, laugh, or whisper in front of the patient.
How to Interact with a Delusional Patient:
✅ DO: "I understand you believe that, but I see it differently." Don't reinforce OR directly challenge the delusion. Focus on the feeling behind the delusion ("You seem frightened"). Redirect to reality-based topics.
❌ DON'T: Argue about the delusion. Present logical evidence against it (won't work). Pretend to agree.

Psychotic Disorder Spectrum — Diagnosing the Differences

DisorderDurationKey Distinguishing Features
Brief Psychotic Disorder1 day – 1 monthOften after severe stress. Full return to premorbid functioning.
Schizophreniform1–6 monthsIdentical to schizophrenia but shorter. May or may not progress.
Schizophrenia≥6 months≥2 symptoms for ≥1 month (active phase) + 6 months total. Chronic, relapsing.
SchizoaffectiveOngoingSchizophrenia + mood disorder (depression or mania) occurring simultaneously.
Delusional Disorder≥1 monthNon-bizarre delusions ONLY. No hallucinations, no disorganized behavior. Functions normally aside from delusion.
NCLEX Key — Schizophrenia Diagnosis: Requires ≥2 of the following for ≥1 month: delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, disorganized/catatonic behavior, negative symptoms. Total duration of illness ≥6 months (including prodromal/residual). If <6 months → schizophreniform. If <1 month → brief psychotic disorder.
5 🎭 Personality Disorders
Remember the Clusters:
Cluster A = "Weird" (odd/eccentric) — Paranoid, Schizoid, Schizotypal
Cluster B = "Wild" (dramatic/emotional) — Antisocial, Borderline, Histrionic, Narcissistic
Cluster C = "Worried" (anxious/fearful) — Avoidant, Dependent, Obsessive-Compulsive

🧊 Cluster A — Odd/Eccentric ("Weird")

DisorderCore PatternKey Features
ParanoidPervasive distrust & suspicionSuspects others of exploiting, deceiving. Reads hidden threats into benign remarks. Won't confide. Bears grudges. Questions fidelity of spouse.
SchizoidDetachment from social relationshipsPrefers solitude. Emotionally cold/flat. No desire for close relationships. Indifferent to praise/criticism. "Loner" — but NOT distressed by it.
SchizotypalEccentric behavior + perceptual distortionsMagical thinking, ideas of reference, odd beliefs, peculiar speech/dress. Social anxiety that doesn't diminish with familiarity. Closest to schizophrenia without psychosis.

🔥 Cluster B — Dramatic/Emotional ("Wild")

DisorderCore PatternKey Features
AntisocialDisregard for rights of othersDeceitful, impulsive, aggressive, irresponsible. No remorse. Must be ≥18 with conduct disorder history before 15. "Charming manipulator."
Borderline (BPD)Instability in relationships, self-image, affectSplitting (all good vs. all bad), fear of abandonment, impulsive self-harm, chronic emptiness, identity disturbance, intense/unstable relationships.
HistrionicExcessive emotionality & attention-seekingUncomfortable when not center of attention. Dramatic, seductive, shallow emotions. "Theater personality" — uses appearance to draw attention.
NarcissisticGrandiosity, need for admiration, lack of empathyEntitled, exploitative, envious, arrogant. Fragile self-esteem beneath grandiosity. Reacts to criticism with rage or shame.

😰 Cluster C — Anxious/Fearful ("Worried")

DisorderCore PatternKey Features
AvoidantSocial inhibition, fear of rejectionDesperately WANTS relationships but avoids them due to fear of inadequacy. Hypersensitive to criticism. Unlike schizoid — they DO feel distress.
DependentExcessive need to be cared forCan't make decisions alone, needs reassurance, fears separation, goes to excessive lengths to obtain nurturance. Submissive, clingy.
OCPDPreoccupation with orderliness, perfectionism, controlRigid, inflexible about rules. Workaholic. Ego-syntonic (feels natural to them). Different from OCD which is ego-dystonic!
Nursing Approach — Borderline Patient:
✅ DO: Be consistent, set clear boundaries, use matter-of-fact tone. Recognize splitting behavior ("You're the only good nurse" / "All nurses are terrible"). Validate feelings without reinforcing maladaptive behavior. Use DBT principles (Dialectical Behavior Therapy).
❌ DON'T: Take splitting personally. Become the "special" nurse. Make promises you can't keep. React to manipulation with anger. The entire care team must be consistent.
NCLEX Key — OCD vs. OCPD: OCD = ego-dystonic (patient recognizes their obsessions are irrational and distressing). OCPD = ego-syntonic (patient sees nothing wrong — "everyone SHOULD be this organized"). OCD is an anxiety disorder. OCPD is a personality disorder.
6 🍷 Substance Use & Withdrawal

Intoxication vs. Withdrawal — Key Comparisons

SubstanceIntoxication SignsWithdrawal SignsTreatment
AlcoholSlurred speech, ataxia, disinhibition, nystagmus, stuporTremors → seizures → DTs (24-72h). Hallucinations, tachycardia, diaphoresis, agitation. Can be fatal!Benzodiazepines (Chlordiazepoxide, Lorazepam), thiamine BEFORE glucose, IV fluids, seizure precautions
OpioidsPinpoint pupils, respiratory depression, euphoria, sedation, constipationDilated pupils, rhinorrhea, lacrimation, N/V/D, yawning, muscle aches, piloerection ("cold turkey")Intox: Naloxone (Narcan). Withdrawal: Methadone, Buprenorphine, Clonidine
BenzodiazepinesSimilar to alcohol: sedation, confusion, amnesia, respiratory depressionSimilar to alcohol: seizures possible. Can be fatal! Anxiety, insomnia, tremors, perceptual disturbancesIntox: Flumazenil. Withdrawal: slow taper
Stimulants (Cocaine/Amphetamines)Euphoria, tachycardia, hypertension, dilated pupils, hyperthermia, paranoia, aggression"Crash": severe depression, fatigue, hypersomnia, increased appetite. NOT physically dangerous but high suicide riskSupportive. Intox: Benzodiazepines for agitation. No specific antidote.
CannabisEuphoria, relaxed, ↑appetite, red conjunctivae, dry mouth, impaired coordinationIrritability, insomnia, decreased appetite. Mild, not dangerousSupportive
Hallucinogens (LSD/PCP)LSD: visual hallucinations, synesthesia, dilated pupils. PCP: nystagmus, violence, numbnessNo significant physical withdrawalCalm environment, "talk down." PCP: minimize stimuli, isolate if violent
Only TWO withdrawals can KILL: Alcohol and Benzodiazepines. Both can cause fatal seizures and require medical detox. Opioid withdrawal is miserable but rarely fatal (exception: pregnant women — risk of fetal distress).
Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome:
Wernicke's (acute, reversible): Confusion + Ataxia + Ophthalmoplegia (eye paralysis) — "CAO"
Korsakoff's (chronic, irreversible): Confabulation (making up stories to fill memory gaps) + amnesia
Prevention: Give Thiamine (B1) BEFORE glucose in all alcoholics! Glucose without thiamine → Wernicke's.

🪶 Anorexia Nervosa VS 🔄 Bulimia Nervosa

WeightSignificantly underweight (BMI <18.5). Refuses to maintain normal weight.Usually normal weight or slightly overweight
PatternSevere restriction ± binge/purge typeBinge-purge cycles. Eats large amounts then compensates (vomiting, laxatives, exercise)
Self-ImageDistorted body image — sees self as fat even when emaciatedAware behavior is abnormal. Shame and secrecy about bingeing.

Refeeding Syndrome — Potentially Fatal! When severely malnourished patients begin eating again, rapid shifts in electrolytes (especially ↓ phosphorus) can cause cardiac failure, respiratory failure, and death. Refeed SLOWLY. Monitor phosphorus, K+, Mg++. Cardiac monitoring first 72 hours.
Nursing Approach — Anorexia Patient:
✅ DO: Monitor meals (sit with patient during AND 1 hour after). Daily weights (same time, same scale, gown only). Strict I&O. Monitor cardiac rhythm. Set weight gain goals (1-2 lbs/week). Focus conversations on feelings, NOT food/weight.
❌ DON'T: Allow bathroom unsupervised after meals (purging risk). Discuss calories/food amounts. Let patient prepare own meals initially. Use food as reward/punishment.
8 ⚠️ Suicide Assessment & Prevention

⚠️ Suicide Risk Assessment

🚨 CRITICAL SAFETY

Risk Factors — SAD PERSONS Scale:

  • Sex (male completes more; female attempts more)
  • Age (elderly >65 highest completion rate; adolescents highest attempt rate)
  • Depression (or hopelessness — strongest predictor)
  • Previous attempt (#1 predictor of future attempt)
  • Ethanol/substance abuse
  • Rational thinking loss (psychosis)
  • Social supports lacking (isolated, divorced, widowed)
  • Organized plan (specific method, access to means, timeline)
  • No spouse / significant other
  • Sickness (chronic illness, chronic pain, terminal diagnosis)

Warning Signs — ACE Mnemonic:

  • Attempt (previous attempt or planning)
  • Current plan with means and timeline
  • Expressions of hopelessness, burden, or trapped feeling
NCLEX Key — Always Ask Directly: "Are you thinking about killing yourself?" Asking about suicide does NOT plant the idea. It gives permission to discuss it. If yes → assess plan, means, intent, timeline. More specific plan = higher risk.

Nursing Interventions for Suicidal Patient

PriorityAction
1. Safety1:1 continuous observation. Remove ALL dangerous items: belts, shoelaces, sharps, glass, cords, plastic bags. Check belongings on admission.
2. EnvironmentPlace near nurses' station. Locked windows. No-suicide contract (in orientation phase). Break-away fixtures in bathroom.
3. CommunicationUse direct language. "Are you thinking of hurting yourself?" Document specific quotes. Active listening without judgment.
4. MedicationMonitor for cheek/hoarding pills. Liquid forms preferred. SSRI black box warning: increased suicidality in <25 y/o in first weeks
5. DischargeSafety plan, emergency contacts, remove means at home, follow-up within 72 hours. Involve family with patient consent.
9 💊 Psychopharmacology

Antidepressants

ClassKey DrugsMechanismCritical Nursing Points
SSRIsFluoxetine (Prozac), Sertraline (Zoloft), Paroxetine (Paxil), Escitalopram (Lexapro)Block serotonin reuptakeTakes 2-4 weeks for effect. Sexual dysfunction common. Serotonin syndrome risk with MAOIs. First-line for depression, anxiety, OCD, PTSD
SNRIsVenlafaxine (Effexor), Duloxetine (Cymbalta)Block serotonin + norepinephrine reuptakeMay ↑BP. Good for depression + chronic pain. Taper slowly — withdrawal syndrome
TCAsAmitriptyline, Nortriptyline, ImipramineBlock serotonin + norepinephrine reuptake (non-selective)Lethal in overdose (cardiac arrhythmias). Anticholinergic effects. Not first-line due to toxicity. Never give full supply to suicidal patient!
MAOIsPhenelzine (Nardil), Tranylcypromine (Parnate)Inhibit monoamine oxidase (breaks down serotonin, NE, dopamine)Tyramine crisis! Avoid aged cheese, wine, smoked meats, soy sauce, beer → hypertensive crisis. 14-day washout between MAOIs and SSRIs.
Serotonin Syndrome — Life-Threatening Emergency! Caused by excess serotonin (combining SSRIs + MAOIs, SSRIs + triptans, etc.).
Triad: Altered mental status + Autonomic instability (↑HR, ↑BP, ↑Temp, diaphoresis) + Neuromuscular hyperactivity (clonus, hyperreflexia, tremor, rigidity).
Treatment: Stop all serotonergic drugs. Cyproheptadine (serotonin antagonist). Cooling measures. Supportive care.

Mood Stabilizers

💎 Lithium — The Gold Standard for Bipolar

NARROW THERAPEUTIC INDEX TOXICITY RISK

Therapeutic level: 0.6–1.2 mEq/L. Draw trough levels (12 hours after last dose).

Early Toxicity (1.5–2.0):

  • N/V, diarrhea, coarse tremor, drowsiness, muscle weakness, slurred speech

Severe Toxicity (2.0–3.5):

  • Ataxia, confusion, seizures, cardiac arrhythmias, renal failure, coma, death

Critical Nursing Points:

  • Monitor levels weekly initially, then monthly when stable
  • Maintain adequate sodium and fluid intake — dehydration concentrates lithium
  • NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, thiazide diuretics ↑ lithium levels
  • Monitor thyroid (hypothyroidism) and renal function — lithium is nephrotoxic long-term
  • Pregnancy category D — Ebstein's anomaly (cardiac defect)

Antipsychotics

ClassKey DrugsSide EffectsKey Points
Typical (1st gen)Haloperidol (Haldol), Chlorpromazine (Thorazine)EPS: dystonia, akathisia, parkinsonism, tardive dyskinesia. NMS (neuroleptic malignant syndrome)Good for positive symptoms. High EPS risk. Benztropine (Cogentin) or Diphenhydramine for EPS.

Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS) — Medical Emergency!
FEVER mnemonic: Fever (↑ to 107°F) + Encephalopathy (confusion) + Vitals unstable (↑BP, ↑HR) + Elevated enzymes (CPK markedly ↑) + Rigidity (lead-pipe)
Action: STOP antipsychotic immediately. Dantrolene (muscle relaxant) + Bromocriptine (dopamine agonist). Cooling measures. ICU monitoring.

Benzodiazepines (Lorazepam, Diazepam, Alprazolam)Acute anxiety, panic, seizures, alcohol withdrawal, insomniaDependence risk! Short-term use only. CNS depression with alcohol. Antidote: Flumazenil. Elderly: ↑ fall risk.

10 ⚖️ Legal, Ethical & Safety

Admission Types

TypeHow It WorksKey Points
VoluntaryPatient requests admission and signs consentCan leave AMA (with notice period, usually 72 hrs). Retains all rights. Preferred when possible.
InvoluntaryCourt-ordered or emergency hold. Danger to self or others or gravely disabled.Duration varies by state (usually 72-hr hold for evaluation). Patient retains right to refuse treatment EXCEPT in emergencies.
EmergencyImmediate danger. Physician can initiate.Shortest hold. Purely for stabilization and evaluation. Converted to voluntary or involuntary as needed.

Patient Rights in Psychiatric Settings

  • Right to refuse treatment (including medication) — UNLESS imminent danger to self/others
  • Right to informed consent — explained risks, benefits, alternatives
  • Right to least restrictive environment
  • Right to confidentiality — exceptions: duty to warn (Tarasoff), child/elder abuse reporting, court orders
  • Right to communicate — phone, mail, visitors (may be limited for safety)
  • Right to be free from restraints unless danger is imminent and all alternatives exhausted

Restraints — Use as Last Resort

🔒 Restraint Guidelines

LAST RESORT ONLY

Order of interventions: Verbal de-escalation → PRN medication → 1:1 observation → seclusion → physical restraints (LAST).

When restraints are applied:

  • Physician order required within 1 hour of application
  • Behavioral health: reassess every 4 hours (adults), 2 hours (children 9-17), 1 hour (under 9)
  • Check circulation, sensation, movement (CSM) every 15 minutes
  • Offer food, fluids, toileting every 2 hours
  • Remove restraints as soon as patient is safe — reassess continuously
  • Document: behavior requiring restraint, alternatives attempted, patient response
NCLEX Key — Duty to Warn (Tarasoff Rule): If a patient makes a credible, specific threat against an identifiable person, the clinician has a legal duty to warn the potential victim AND notify authorities. Confidentiality is broken in this case. This overrides HIPAA.

Communicating with Dementia Patients:
✅ DO: Simple, short sentences. One-step commands. Identify yourself each visit. Use calm, reassuring tone. Approach from front. Offer choices (not open-ended questions). Reality orientation in early stages only.
❌ DON'T: Argue about what's real. Say "Don't you remember?" Use logic or reason. Use reality orientation in late stages (causes distress). Restrain for wandering (use redirection, alarms, safe walking areas).
Sundowner's Syndrome: Increased confusion and agitation in late afternoon/evening. Nursing: increase lighting, maintain routine, avoid caffeine after noon, provide calming activities in afternoon, assess for fatigue/pain.

14 🧠 High-Yield NCLEX Memory Anchors

🗣️ Communication

  • Best answer = acknowledges feelings + encourages elaboration
  • Eliminate: false reassurance, advice, "why?"
  • Silence IS therapeutic

😰 Anxiety Levels

  • Mild = BEST for teaching
  • Panic = CANNOT learn → safety only
  • Moderate = most common testing level

💔 Depression Safety

  • Suicide risk ↑ when energy RETURNS
  • SIG E CAPS = diagnostic criteria
  • Give away possessions = red flag

🔮 Schizophrenia

  • Positive = added (hallucinations, delusions)
  • Negative = subtracted (5 A's)
  • ≥6 months = schizophrenia diagnosis

🎭 Personality Clusters

  • A = Weird (paranoid, schizoid, schizotypal)
  • B = Wild (antisocial, borderline, histrionic, narcissistic)
  • C = Worried (avoidant, dependent, OCPD)

🍷 Substance Withdrawal

  • Only alcohol & benzos can KILL on withdrawal
  • Thiamine BEFORE glucose in alcoholics
  • Opioid withdrawal = miserable but NOT fatal

💊 Lithium

  • Level: 0.6–1.2 mEq/L
  • Toxicity: tremor → confusion → seizures → death
  • Maintain Na+ and fluids. Avoid NSAIDs

⚡ Delirium vs. Dementia

  • Delirium = ACUTE, reversible, find the cause
  • Dementia = gradual, irreversible, safety focus
  • #1 cause of delirium in elderly = UTI
Final NCLEX Tip: Psychiatric nursing questions test therapeutic communication first, safety second, and medication knowledge third. When unsure, choose the answer that acknowledges feelings and maintains safety. Always assess before intervening. Never argue with delusions or hallucinations — present reality gently. And remember: the nurse-patient relationship is the foundation of ALL psychiatric care.
