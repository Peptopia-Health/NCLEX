🗣️ Therapeutic Communication

Core

Core Principles (NCLEX Gold)

Therapeutic communication is the foundation of psychiatric nursing. The nurse uses purposeful, goal-directed communication to promote the client's well-being and encourage expression of feelings.

  • Open-ended questions: Encourage the client to elaborate ("Tell me about your experience...")
  • Reflection: Mirror the client's feelings back to them ("You seem angry about that.")
  • Restating: Repeat the client's words to confirm understanding ("So you're saying you feel hopeless?")
  • Silence: Allow the client time to think and process emotions without interrupting
  • Acknowledging: Recognize the client's feelings without judgment ("I can see this is difficult for you.")
  • Clarifying: Seek to understand vague or confusing statements ("What do you mean by that?")
  • Focusing: Direct the conversation to important topics ("Let's talk more about what happened yesterday.")

DO / DON'T Interaction Boxes

DO

"Tell me more about how you're feeling."

DON'T

"Don't worry, everything will be fine." (false reassurance)

DO

"It sounds like you're feeling scared." (reflection)

DON'T

"Why did you do that?" (judgmental, puts client on defensive)

DO

Use silence after emotional statements — give the client space to process.

DON'T

Change the subject or offer false reassurance when the client expresses pain.

SOLER — Sit squarely facing client, Open posture, Lean forward slightly, Eye contact (culturally appropriate), Relax — the body language of active listening.
NCLEX loves "Tell me more" — when in doubt, pick the response that encourages the client to talk. The best answer is almost always the one that explores the client's feelings further.

😰 Anxiety Spectrum Disorders

Core

Levels of Anxiety

MildAlert, perceptual field broad, learning enhancedSlight muscle tension, restlessnessMotivated, attentive, asks questions

Nursing Interventions per Level

  • Mild: Use for teaching moments — client learns best at this level
  • Moderate: Direct attention, help client make connections, use clear and simple statements
  • Severe: Reduce stimuli, stay with client, give simple directions, do NOT try to teach
  • Panic: SAFETY first — stay with client, reduce all stimuli, use calm simple words, provide safe environment
Severe/Panic = SAFETY first. Reduce stimuli, stay with client, simple directions. Do NOT attempt to teach or reason with a panicking patient.

Anxiety Disorders Overview

  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD): Excessive worry about everyday things for 6+ months; difficulty controlling worry; fatigue, muscle tension, irritability, sleep disturbance
  • Panic Disorder: Recurrent unexpected panic attacks; fear of future attacks; may develop agoraphobia
  • Phobias: Irrational, persistent fear of specific object/situation; treatment includes systematic desensitization (gradual exposure) and CBT
  • OCD: Obsessions (intrusive thoughts) and compulsions (repetitive behaviors to reduce anxiety); very distressing to client
  • PTSD: Follows exposure to traumatic event; intrusive memories/flashbacks, hyperarousal, avoidance of triggers, emotional numbing
OCD Nursing: "Don't STOP the ritual" — do not interrupt compulsive behavior. Allow time for rituals, then redirect. Interrupting increases anxiety dramatically.

PTSD Key Points

  • Three symptom clusters: intrusive memories (flashbacks, nightmares), hyperarousal (exaggerated startle, insomnia, irritability), avoidance (emotional numbing, detachment)
  • Avoid known triggers during treatment; promote safe expression of feelings
  • Treatment: trauma-focused CBT, EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing), group therapy
  • Medications: SSRIs (first-line), prazosin for nightmares

💔 Mood Disorders

Core

Major Depressive Disorder

SIG E CAPS — Sleep changes, Interest loss (anhedonia), Guilt/worthlessness, Energy decreased, Concentration impaired, Appetite changes, Psychomotor changes, Suicidal ideation. Need 5+ symptoms for 2+ weeks including depressed mood or anhedonia.
  • Nursing priorities: Safety (suicide risk), establish trust, promote self-care, administer medications, monitor for improvement
  • Depression often presents as irritability in adolescents and somatic complaints in older adults
  • Avoid saying "cheer up" or "snap out of it" — these are non-therapeutic and belittling

PsychosisCan occur during manic episodesNo psychotic features

DIGFAST for Mania — Distractibility, Insomnia (decreased need for sleep), Grandiosity, Flight of ideas, Activity increased, Speech pressured, Thoughtlessness (poor judgment, risky behavior)

Suicide Risk in Depression

Suicide risk is HIGHEST during the recovery phase — when energy is returning but the plan is already formed. A suddenly "cheerful" depressed client may have decided to act. INCREASE monitoring during this period.

Lithium

  • Therapeutic range: 0.6 - 1.2 mEq/L (draw 8-12 hours after last dose)
  • Early toxicity (1.5-2.0): nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, coarse tremors, drowsiness
  • Advanced toxicity (2.0-3.0): ataxia, blurred vision, tinnitus, muscle twitching, severe hypotension
  • Severe toxicity (>3.0): seizures, coma, cardiac dysrhythmias, death
  • Monitoring: thyroid function (hypothyroidism risk), renal function (kidney excretes lithium), weight, ECG
  • Drug interactions: NSAIDs and ACE inhibitors increase lithium levels; caffeine and theophylline decrease levels
Lithium and sodium relationship — low Na+ = lithium toxicity. Client must maintain adequate sodium and fluid intake. Avoid dehydration (vomiting, diarrhea, excessive sweating, diuretics).

ECT (Electroconvulsive Therapy)

  • Indications: Severe depression unresponsive to medications, acute mania, catatonia, active suicidality needing rapid response
  • Pre-ECT: NPO 6-8 hours, signed consent, baseline vital signs, remove dentures/jewelry, void, atropine (decrease secretions), short-acting anesthetic + succinylcholine (muscle relaxant)
  • Post-ECT: Side-lying until gag reflex returns, monitor vitals, reorient (confusion is expected), assess for headache, short-term memory loss is common

🔮 Schizophrenia & Psychotic Disorders

Amplified

Positive vs Negative Symptoms

Positive symptoms = EXCESS (added to normal behavior) — hallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech, bizarre behavior. Negative symptoms = DEFICIT (taken away from normal) — flat affect, alogia, anhedonia, avolition, social withdrawal.

Positive Symptoms (Excess)

  • Hallucinations: False sensory perceptions
  • Delusions: Fixed false beliefs
  • Disorganized speech: Loose associations, word salad, tangentiality, neologisms
  • Bizarre behavior: Catatonia, waxy flexibility, echolalia, echopraxia
  • Respond better to antipsychotics

Negative Symptoms (Deficit)

  • Flat affect: No emotional expression
  • Alogia: Poverty of speech
  • Anhedonia: No pleasure in activities
  • Avolition: Lack of motivation
  • Social withdrawal: Isolation from others
  • Harder to treat; atypical antipsychotics may help

How to Interact with Psychotic Patients

DO — Hallucinations

"I don't hear the voices, but I understand they are real to you. What are they saying?" Acknowledge the experience, assess for danger, redirect to reality-based activity.

DON'T — Hallucinations

Do NOT argue about reality ("There are no voices!") or pretend to hear what the client hears. Never agree with the hallucination content.

DO — Delusions

"I understand you believe that. I see it differently. Let's focus on your goals for today." Acknowledge feelings, do not reinforce, redirect to reality.

DON'T — Delusions

Do NOT reinforce the delusion ("Tell me more about the CIA") or directly challenge it ("That's crazy, the CIA isn't watching you"). Both approaches are non-therapeutic.

DO — Paranoia

Be consistent, transparent, and reliable. Use simple clear language. Offer sealed food containers. Avoid touching without permission. Build trust slowly.

DON'T — Paranoia

Do NOT whisper near the patient, laugh without explanation, have private conversations in the client's view, or make promises you cannot keep. These reinforce paranoid thinking.

Command hallucinations: SAFETY PRIORITY — always assess for intent to harm self or others. If the voices are commanding the client to hurt themselves or others, implement 1:1 observation immediately and notify the provider.

NMS (Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome)

NMS is LIFE-THREATENING! FEVER acronym: Fever (extreme, 106+°F), Encephalopathy (altered mental status), Vitals unstable (tachycardia, labile BP), Elevated CPK and WBC, Rigidity (lead-pipe). STOP all antipsychotics immediately. Treatment: dantrolene IV and bromocriptine. Supportive care: cooling blankets, IV fluids, monitor renal function.

Clozapine Special Monitoring

  • ANC monitoring: Absolute Neutrophil Count must be checked regularly through the Clozapine REMS program
  • Weekly ANC for first 6 months, then biweekly for 6 months, then monthly if stable
  • Agranulocytosis risk: If ANC <1500, hold medication; if ANC <500, discontinue and do NOT rechallenge
  • Report sore throat, fever, malaise immediately — may indicate neutropenia
  • Also causes significant sedation, weight gain, metabolic syndrome, seizure risk, myocarditis

🎭 Personality Disorders

Core
Cluster A = Weird (odd/eccentric), Cluster B = Wild (dramatic/erratic), Cluster C = Worried (anxious/fearful)

Cluster A — Odd/Eccentric

Cluster A Personality Disorders

  • Paranoid PD: Pervasive distrust and suspicion of others; interprets motives as malevolent; reluctant to confide; bears grudges; reads threatening meanings into benign events
  • Schizoid PD: Detachment from social relationships; restricted emotional expression; prefers solitary activities; indifferent to praise or criticism; appears cold and aloof
  • Schizotypal PD: Eccentric behavior, magical thinking, odd beliefs, unusual perceptual experiences; social anxiety that does not improve with familiarity; ideas of reference

Nursing: Build trust slowly, respect need for distance, be clear and consistent, avoid misinterpretation of intent

Cluster B — Dramatic/Erratic

Cluster B Personality Disorders

  • Antisocial PD: Disregard for rights of others, deceitfulness, impulsivity, aggression, lack of remorse, manipulation; must be 18+ with history of conduct disorder before age 15
  • Borderline PD: Instability in relationships, self-image, affects; marked impulsivity; frantic efforts to avoid abandonment; splitting (all good vs all bad); chronic emptiness; self-harm; intense anger
  • Histrionic PD: Excessive emotionality and attention-seeking; uncomfortable when not center of attention; uses physical appearance to draw attention; considers relationships more intimate than they are
  • Narcissistic PD: Grandiosity, need for admiration, lack of empathy; sense of entitlement; exploitative of others; envious; arrogant; fragile self-esteem beneath the surface

Nursing: Set firm, consistent limits; avoid power struggles; maintain boundaries; document behaviors; team approach to prevent splitting

Cluster C — Anxious/Fearful

Cluster C Personality Disorders

  • Avoidant PD: Social inhibition, feelings of inadequacy, hypersensitivity to criticism; avoids activities involving interpersonal contact; views self as inferior; reluctant to take risks
  • Dependent PD: Excessive need to be taken care of; submissive and clinging behavior; difficulty making decisions; fear of separation; urgently seeks another relationship when one ends
  • Obsessive-Compulsive PD: Preoccupation with orderliness, perfectionism, control; rigid and inflexible; workaholic; reluctant to delegate; miserly. Note: different from OCD (which is an anxiety disorder)

Nursing: Encourage independence, build self-esteem, promote assertiveness skills, support decision-making

Borderline PD Deep Dive

  • Splitting: Sees people as all good or all bad; can split staff members ("You're the only good nurse here")
  • Self-harm: Cutting, burning — often used to regulate emotions, not necessarily suicidal (but always assess)
  • Abandonment fear: May exhibit desperate behaviors when perceiving rejection (threats, clinging, rage)
  • Treatment: Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) — teaches distress tolerance, emotional regulation, mindfulness, interpersonal effectiveness
  • Nursing: Consistent limits, same staff when possible, avoid favoritism, team communication to prevent splitting, matter-of-fact approach

Antisocial PD Key Points

  • Master manipulators — will test limits, pit staff against each other, charm to get what they want
  • Lack of remorse or guilt; do not learn from consequences
  • Nursing: Set FIRM limits and enforce consistently; document manipulation attempts; team approach; avoid being "special" to the client; maintain professional boundaries

🍷 Substance Use & Withdrawal

Core

Alcohol Withdrawal Timeline

Time After Last DrinkSymptomsSeverity
6-24 hoursTremors, anxiety, insomnia, nausea, tachycardia, diaphoresis, elevated BPMild to moderate
24-48 hoursHallucinations (auditory, visual, tactile); may hear/see things but knows they are not real (unlike DTs)Moderate

Alcohol and benzodiazepine withdrawal CAN be FATAL (seizures, DTs). These are the two most dangerous substance withdrawals. Opioid withdrawal is miserable but NOT life-threatening.

CIWA Protocol (Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment)

  • Standardized 10-item scale to assess alcohol withdrawal severity
  • Assesses: nausea, tremor, anxiety, agitation, paroxysmal sweats, visual/auditory/tactile disturbances, headache, orientation
  • Score <10: mild, monitor; 10-18: moderate, consider medication; >20: severe, medicate and monitor closely
  • Allows symptom-triggered dosing of benzodiazepines — more effective than fixed-dose schedules

Opioid Emergencies

  • Opioid withdrawal: Flu-like symptoms — rhinorrhea, lacrimation, yawning, muscle aches, diarrhea, goosebumps, abdominal cramps. Miserable but NOT life-threatening.
  • Opioid overdose: Classic triad — pinpoint pupils, respiratory depression, decreased LOC. Treatment: naloxone (Narcan) — may need repeat doses (Narcan wears off faster than opioids)

Benzodiazepine Overdose

  • Signs: excessive sedation, respiratory depression, hypotension, confusion, coma
  • Antidote: flumazenil (Romazicon) — use cautiously; may precipitate seizures in dependent patients

Stimulant Use

IntoxicationWithdrawal
SignsEuphoria, tachycardia, hypertension, dilated pupils, hyperthermia, agitation, paranoia, seizuresDepression, fatigue, increased appetite, hypersomnia, psychomotor slowing, intense cravings
NursingCalm environment, reduce stimuli, monitor cardiac status, seizure precautions, cooling measuresSupportive care, ensure safety (suicide risk from depression), promote nutrition and rest

Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome

  • Caused by chronic thiamine (B1) deficiency from prolonged alcohol abuse
  • Wernicke's encephalopathy (acute, reversible): confusion, ataxia, ophthalmoplegia (nystagmus, paralysis of eye muscles)
  • Korsakoff's psychosis (chronic, often irreversible): severe amnesia, confabulation (making up stories to fill memory gaps — they believe it is true)
  • Treatment: IV thiamine BEFORE glucose (giving glucose without thiamine can worsen Wernicke's)
  • Nursing: redirect the patient; do NOT confront confabulation

Core

Anorexia Nervosa vs Bulimia Nervosa

FeatureAnorexia NervosaBulimia Nervosa
Body imageDistorted — sees self as fat despite emaciationDistorted — aware of abnormal eating pattern
WeightSignificantly underweight (<85% expected)Normal or slightly overweight
BehaviorsRestriction, excessive exercise, rituals with foodBinge-purge cycles (vomiting, laxatives, diuretics)

Refeeding syndrome = life-threatening. When malnourished patients begin eating again, phosphorus drops rapidly causing cardiac arrhythmias, respiratory failure, seizures. Monitor phosphorus, potassium, and magnesium closely. Start feeding SLOWLY.

Nursing Interventions

  • Bulimia: Monitor client for 1-2 hours after meals to prevent purging; lock bathroom access after meals; observe for hiding food or secret exercise
  • Anorexia: Weigh with back to scale (to reduce focus on numbers); daily weights, same time, same clothing; monitor I&O; set caloric goals
  • Establish behavioral contracts around meal expectations
  • Avoid power struggles over food — use matter-of-fact approach
  • Family therapy (especially for adolescents), CBT, nutritional counseling

Lab Abnormalities

  • Bulimia: Hypokalemia (from vomiting/laxatives), metabolic alkalosis, elevated amylase (from purging)
  • Anorexia: Amenorrhea, anemia, leukopenia, hypoglycemia, decreased albumin, electrolyte imbalances, elevated BUN

⚠️ Suicide Assessment & Prevention

Core

Risk Factors

  • Demographics: Males complete more; females attempt more; elderly white males = highest completion rate; adolescents have rising rates
  • Previous attempts: Single strongest predictor of future attempts
  • Mental illness: Depression, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, substance abuse, personality disorders (especially borderline)
  • Substance abuse: Significantly increases impulsivity and lethality
  • Social factors: Recent loss, isolation, divorce, unemployment, access to means (firearms)
  • Plan specificity: The more detailed the plan, the higher the risk — especially if they have the means available

Protective Factors

  • Strong social support, family connectedness, religious/cultural beliefs against suicide
  • Access to mental health care, sense of purpose, children in the home, problem-solving skills

Lethality Assessment

  • Method: Firearms > hanging > jumping > drowning > poisoning > cutting (firearms = most lethal)
  • Plan: How specific? Time, place, method determined?
  • Means: Does the client have access to the planned method?
  • Timeline: Immediate plan = highest risk; "someday" = lower but still concerning
  • Safety contracts: Have limitations — not a substitute for proper assessment and monitoring; NOT evidence-based as a sole intervention

Nursing Interventions

  • 1:1 observation: For high-risk clients; continuous, arm's length monitoring
  • Environmental safety: Remove sharps, belts, cords, shoelaces, glass objects, plastic bags, hoarded medications
  • Therapeutic communication: listen actively, take all statements seriously, convey empathy
  • Document level of risk, plan, and interventions
  • Post-attempt care: Assess and treat physical injuries FIRST (medical stability), then address psychological needs
ALWAYS ask directly about suicidal ideation — asking does NOT plant the idea. Direct question: "Are you thinking about hurting yourself or ending your life?" This demonstrates concern and opens the door for honest communication.
RED FLAGS: Giving away prized possessions + sudden mood improvement in a depressed client = client may have made decision to act and feels relief. INCREASE monitoring immediately.

Amplified

DrugKey Features

TYRAMINE CRISIS (Hypertensive Emergency): MAOIs prevent breakdown of tyramine, causing massive norepinephrine release. Signs: severe hypertension, occipital headache, stiff neck, nausea, diaphoresis, palpitations. Can lead to stroke or death. Treatment: phentolamine IV.

Tyramine-Rich Foods to AVOID with MAOIs

  • Aged cheese (cheddar, Swiss, Parmesan — cottage cheese and mozzarella are OK)
  • Cured/smoked meats (salami, pepperoni, hot dogs, bacon, organ meats)
  • Soy sauce, miso, tofu
  • Red wine, beer, tap/draft beer
  • Pickled/fermented foods (sauerkraut, kimchi)
  • Fava beans (broad beans)
  • Overripe fruit (especially bananas, avocados, raisins)
  • Must wait 2 weeks between stopping MAOI and starting SSRI (or vice versa — "washout period")
MAOI + tyramine = hypertensive CRISIS — avoid aged, cured, fermented foods. Remember: if it's aged, smoked, pickled, or fermented, say NO!

lorazepam (Ativan)Intermediate-acting; preferred in liver disease; used for acute anxiety, alcohol withdrawal, seizures
diazepam (Valium)Long-acting; active metabolites; muscle relaxant; alcohol withdrawal; status epilepticus
alprazolam (Xanax)Short-acting; rapid onset; high abuse potential; panic disorder; difficult to taper
  • Short-term use only (2-4 weeks); dependence develops quickly
  • Do NOT stop abruptly — taper to prevent withdrawal seizures
  • CNS depressant effects potentiated by alcohol, opioids — respiratory depression risk
  • Antidote: flumazenil

Buspirone

  • Non-sedating anxiolytic; NO dependence or abuse potential
  • Takes 2-4 weeks for full effect (not for acute anxiety or PRN use)
  • Does not interact with alcohol or CNS depressants
  • Cannot be used to treat benzodiazepine withdrawal (different mechanism)

Typical vs Atypical Antipsychotics

  • High-potency typicals (haloperidol): MORE EPS risk, LESS sedation/anticholinergic effects
  • Low-potency typicals (chlorpromazine): LESS EPS risk, MORE sedation/anticholinergic/orthostatic effects
  • Atypicals (clozapine, risperidone, olanzapine, quetiapine, aripiprazole): Lower EPS risk overall; metabolic syndrome risk (weight gain, diabetes, hyperlipidemia); monitor glucose and lipids

⚖️ Legal, Ethical & Safety

Core

Voluntary vs Involuntary Admission

FeatureVoluntaryInvoluntary
ConsentClient requests admission; signs consentClient does NOT consent; committed by court or physician
CriteriaClient recognizes need for helpDanger to self or others, gravely disabled, unable to provide basic self-care
DischargeClient can request at any time (may require 24-72h notice)Court or treatment team determines readiness; client cannot leave unilaterally
RightsALL patient rights maintainedALL patient rights maintained (rights are NOT suspended)
Client rights are NOT suspended during involuntary admission. Involuntarily committed clients still have the right to refuse treatment (except in emergencies), communicate with attorney, receive visitors, and be treated with dignity.

Informed Consent Capacity

  • Client must demonstrate: understanding of proposed treatment, ability to appreciate situation, ability to reason about options, ability to express a choice
  • Involuntary status does NOT automatically mean client lacks capacity
  • If client lacks capacity: legal guardian or healthcare proxy provides substituted consent

Patient Rights in Psychiatric Settings

  • Right to refuse treatment: Including medications (except in emergencies where client is danger to self/others)
  • Least restrictive environment: Use the least intrusive intervention that ensures safety
  • Confidentiality: Protected under HIPAA; same standards as all healthcare
  • Right to written treatment plan, to participate in treatment decisions, to privacy, to communicate freely

Restraint and Seclusion Guidelines

  • Last resort: Only after less restrictive measures have failed (verbal de-escalation, PRN meds, 1:1, quiet room)
  • Check every 15 minutes: Circulation, sensation, movement, skin integrity, vital signs
  • Release every 2 hours: ROM, toileting, fluids, nutrition, repositioning
  • Physician order required: Must be time-limited (4 hours for adults, 2 hours for ages 9-17, 1 hour for <9 years)
  • Document: Behaviors warranting restraint, alternatives attempted, client response, ongoing assessment
  • Ensure dignity: provide privacy, explain reason, maintain therapeutic relationship

Duty to Warn (Tarasoff)

  • When a client makes a credible threat against a specific, identifiable person, the clinician has a legal duty to warn the intended victim
  • This overrides patient confidentiality
  • Also must notify law enforcement

AMA (Against Medical Advice)

  • Voluntary clients have the right to leave AMA
  • Nursing actions: document decision, educate about risks, ensure discharge medications/instructions, client signs AMA form
  • Involuntary clients cannot leave AMA — if they attempt to leave, it is elopement

5150 / Emergency Detention

  • 72-hour involuntary hold for psychiatric evaluation
  • Criteria: danger to self, danger to others, or gravely disabled (unable to provide food, clothing, shelter)
  • Can be initiated by law enforcement, psychiatrist, or designated mental health professional
  • Must be evaluated by a psychiatrist within the hold period

  • Symptoms must be present before age 12, in 2+ settings, and cause functional impairment
  • Treatment: behavioral interventions + stimulant medications

ADHD Medications

DrugClassKey Points
methylphenidate (Ritalin, Concerta)StimulantFirst-line; give in morning; appetite suppression; monitor growth; drug holidays during summer
amphetamine/dextroamphetamine (Adderall)StimulantGive in morning; insomnia if given late; abuse potential; monitor cardiovascular status
atomoxetine (Strattera)Non-stimulant (NRI)No abuse potential; takes 2-4 weeks; hepatotoxicity risk; may increase suicidal ideation
Monitor growth in children on stimulants — appetite suppression can cause growth suppression. Strategies: give medication AFTER breakfast, high-calorie evening snack, "drug holidays" (off medication during school breaks to allow catch-up growth). Give medication in morning to prevent insomnia.

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)

  • Communication deficits: Delayed or absent speech, difficulty with social reciprocity, impaired nonverbal communication, difficulty understanding abstract language
  • Repetitive behaviors: Stereotyped movements (hand flapping, rocking), insistence on sameness, restricted interests, sensory sensitivities
  • Routine: Changes in routine cause extreme distress — maintain consistency
  • Nursing: structured environment, consistent routine, visual schedules, prepare for transitions, reduce sensory overload, safety precautions (may lack awareness of danger)

Conduct Disorder vs Oppositional Defiant Disorder

FeatureConduct Disorder (CD)Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD)
SeverityMore severe; violates rights of othersLess severe; does not violate rights of others
BehaviorsAggression to people/animals, destruction of property, deceitfulness/theft, serious rule violationsAngry/irritable mood, argumentative/defiant, vindictiveness; but no property destruction or aggression to people/animals
PrognosisMay progress to Antisocial PD in adulthoodMay resolve or progress to CD
TreatmentFirm limits, consistency, family therapy, behavioral programsParent management training, CBT, consistent consequences

Other Important Points

  • Childhood depression: May present as irritability rather than sadness; somatic complaints; declining school performance; social withdrawal
  • Adolescent substance abuse: Screen using CRAFFT tool (Car, Relax, Alone, Forget, Friends, Trouble)
  • Play therapy: Primary therapeutic modality for younger children (under 8); child communicates feelings through play; use art, dolls, puppets, sand tray

Delirium = sudden & reversible (find and fix the cause!). Dementia = gradual & irreversible (manage symptoms, support client and family).

Alzheimer's Disease Stages

StageSymptomsNursing Focus
Early (Mild)Short-term memory loss, difficulty finding words, losing items, poor judgment, personality changes, denialEstablish routine; safety assessment; assist with planning (finances, advance directives); support independence
Middle (Moderate)Wandering, getting lost, aggression, sundowning, incontinence, inability to recognize family (agnosia), cannot perform ADLs (apraxia), aphasiaSafety priority (wander alerts, door alarms); simplify environment; toileting schedule; redirect agitation; validation therapy
Late (Severe)Total dependence; bedbound; nonverbal; does not recognize self; dysphagia; incontinence; contractures; seizuresTotal care; comfort measures; skin integrity; aspiration precautions; palliative/hospice referral; family support

Nursing for Dementia

  • Reorient: Use clocks, calendars, consistent staff; address by name
  • Simplify environment: Reduce clutter, noise, and choices; consistent room arrangement
  • Consistent routine: Same schedule daily; minimize changes; prepare for transitions
  • Validate feelings: Don't argue about reality; use validation therapy ("You miss your mother. Tell me about her.")
  • Use simple, short sentences; one-step commands; yes/no questions

Sundowning Interventions

  • Increased confusion and agitation in late afternoon/evening
  • Increase lighting before sundown; reduce afternoon caffeine; structured afternoon activities
  • Avoid overstimulation in evening; keep routine calm; ensure daytime physical activity
  • Rule out other causes: pain, hunger, full bladder, constipation, medication effects

Medications for Dementia

DrugClassKey Points
donepezil (Aricept)Cholinesterase inhibitorMild to moderate Alzheimer's; give at bedtime; GI side effects (nausea, diarrhea); NOT curative — slows decline
memantine (Namenda)NMDA receptor antagonistModerate to severe Alzheimer's; can combine with donepezil; fewer GI side effects; NOT curative

Caregiver Support

  • Caregiver burnout is a major concern — assess at every visit
  • Refer to Alzheimer's Association, support groups, respite care
  • Educate about disease progression, safety measures, community resources
  • Validate caregiver emotions: grief, guilt, frustration, exhaustion are all normal

Review

Quick-Fire Must-Know Facts

  • "Tell me more" = therapeutic response. When in doubt, pick the answer that encourages the client to elaborate on their feelings.
  • Lithium therapeutic range: 0.6 - 1.2 mEq/L. Draw level 8-12 hours after last dose. Low sodium = lithium toxicity. Monitor thyroid and renal function.
  • Clozapine = ANC monitoring. Weekly for 6 months, biweekly for 6 months, then monthly. Agranulocytosis risk — report sore throat, fever immediately.
  • EPS onset timeline: Hours (dystonia) → Days (akathisia) → Weeks (parkinsonism) → Months (tardive dyskinesia). Remember: "Days After, Weeks After, Months After = DAWM"
  • Alcohol and benzo withdrawal = seizure risk. These are the two substance withdrawals that CAN be fatal. Opioid withdrawal is NOT life-threatening.
  • NMS: Fever + lead-pipe rigidity + altered mental status = EMERGENCY. Stop all antipsychotics. Give dantrolene and bromocriptine. Elevated CPK is a hallmark.
  • MAOIs + tyramine = hypertensive crisis. Avoid aged cheese, cured meats, soy sauce, red wine, pickled foods, fava beans. Antidote: phentolamine.
  • Suicide risk is HIGHEST during recovery — energy returns before mood improves. A suddenly cheerful depressed patient is a RED FLAG. Giving away possessions = RED FLAG.
  • Least restrictive environment: Always use the least intrusive intervention that ensures safety. Restraints are LAST resort.
  • Restraints: check q15 minutes, release q2 hours. Assess circulation, sensation, movement. Document behaviors warranting restraint and alternatives attempted.
  • Delirium = reversible (sudden onset, find the cause). Dementia = irreversible (gradual onset, manage symptoms).
  • Refeeding syndrome: watch phosphorus. Also monitor potassium and magnesium. Start feeding slowly in malnourished patients. Can cause cardiac arrest.
  • Serotonin syndrome: confusion, agitation, diarrhea, hyperthermia, clonus/myoclonus. Differs from NMS: serotonin syndrome has hyperreflexia and GI symptoms; NMS has lead-pipe rigidity and elevated CPK.
  • TCAs are lethal in overdose — cardiac arrhythmias. Limit supply in suicidal patients. SSRIs are much safer.
  • Asking about suicide does NOT plant the idea — always ask directly. "Are you thinking about hurting yourself?"
  • Borderline PD = splitting. Use consistent staff, team communication, firm limits. Treatment of choice: DBT.
  • Command hallucinations = always assess for intent to harm. Priority is SAFETY.
  • CIWA protocol: Standardized assessment for alcohol withdrawal. Score drives benzodiazepine dosing.
  • Therapeutic relationship phases: Pre-interaction → Orientation → Working → Termination. Trust is established in orientation phase.
  • Involuntary admission does NOT remove patient rights. Client can still refuse treatment except in emergencies.
  • Tarasoff = duty to warn. If client threatens a specific person, must break confidentiality to warn the intended victim.
  • Anorexia nervosa has the HIGHEST mortality rate of any psychiatric disorder.
  • Wernicke's: give thiamine BEFORE glucose. Confusion + ataxia + eye changes = thiamine deficiency in alcoholics.
  • Buspirone: No sedation, no dependence, takes 2-4 weeks. NOT for acute anxiety or PRN use. Cannot treat benzo withdrawal.
