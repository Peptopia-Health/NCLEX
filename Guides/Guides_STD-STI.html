STD/STI Study Guide

Comprehensive NCLEX Preparation for Sexually Transmitted Diseases & Infections
NCLEX NURSING PRIORITIES
  • Patient Safety: Prevent transmission and complications
  • Confidentiality: Maintain strict privacy and non-judgmental care
  • Partner Notification: Encourage testing and treatment of sexual partners
  • Patient Education: Treatment compliance, safer sex practices, prevention
  • Screening: High-risk populations, pregnant women, routine screening guidelines
  • Reportable Diseases: Know mandatory reporting requirements (Chlamydia, Gonorrhea, Syphilis, HIV)

Comparative Analysis: Bacterial STIs

Chlamydia

Category Details
Organism Chlamydia trachomatis (obligate intracellular bacterium)
Most Common STI Most frequently reported bacterial STI in the United States
Signs & Symptoms Often asymptomatic (70-80% of women, 50% of men)
Women: Mucopurulent cervical discharge, dysuria, urinary frequency, post-coital bleeding, intermenstrual bleeding, lower abdominal pain
Men: Urethral discharge (clear to white), dysuria, testicular pain, epididymitis
Both: Rectal infection (proctitis), pharyngitis (rare)
Diagnostic Tests • Nucleic Acid Amplification Test (NAAT) - gold standard
• First-catch urine sample or cervical/urethral swab
• Can test urine, endocervical, vaginal, urethral, rectal, or pharyngeal specimens
Treatment First-line: Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID x 7 days
Alternative: Azithromycin 1 g PO single dose (if compliance concern)
Pregnancy: Azithromycin 1 g PO single dose (doxycycline contraindicated)
Complications • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)
• Ectopic pregnancy
• Infertility (tubal scarring)
• Chronic pelvic pain
• Epididymitis in men
• Reactive arthritis (Reiter's syndrome)
• Neonatal conjunctivitis and pneumonia
Nursing Considerations • Treat all sexual partners from past 60 days
• Abstain from sex for 7 days after treatment completed
• Retest in 3 months (high reinfection rate)
• Screen pregnant women at first prenatal visit and 3rd trimester
• Report to public health department
• Encourage safer sex practices (condom use)
Clinical Pearl: Chlamydia Chlamydia is called the "silent disease" because most infections are asymptomatic. This makes routine screening crucial, especially for sexually active women under 25 and pregnant women. Always treat partners simultaneously to prevent "ping-pong" reinfection.

Gonorrhea

Category Details
Organism Neisseria gonorrhoeae (gram-negative diplococcus)
Characteristics Second most common bacterial STI; increasing antibiotic resistance
Signs & Symptoms Women: Often asymptomatic (50%), purulent yellow-green cervical discharge, dysuria, urinary frequency, intermenstrual bleeding, Bartholin's gland abscess
Men: Purulent urethral discharge (yellow-green), severe dysuria, urinary frequency, testicular pain
Disseminated: Fever, petechial or pustular skin lesions, arthritis (septic joint), tenosynovitis
Diagnostic Tests • NAAT (nucleic acid amplification test) - preferred
• Gram stain (men) - see gram-negative intracellular diplococci
• Culture (used for antibiotic resistance testing)
• Test urine, cervical, urethral, rectal, pharyngeal specimens
Treatment Uncomplicated: Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose (≥150 kg: 1 g)
Treat for Chlamydia concurrently: Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID x 7 days (if not ruled out)
Disseminated/PID: Hospitalization may be required, IV antibiotics
Complications • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)
• Infertility and ectopic pregnancy
• Disseminated gonococcal infection (DGI)
• Gonococcal arthritis
• Epididymitis in men
• Neonatal ophthalmia neonatorum (blindness prevention with erythromycin eye ointment at birth)
Nursing Considerations • Often co-infected with Chlamydia - treat both
• Test of cure not routinely recommended unless symptoms persist
• Treat all partners from past 60 days
• Abstain from sex for 7 days after treatment
• Report to public health department
• Monitor for antibiotic resistance trends
• Pregnant women: screen at first visit and 3rd trimester
NCLEX Alert: Antibiotic Resistance Gonorrhea has developed resistance to multiple antibiotics over time. Ceftriaxone IM is currently the only recommended first-line treatment. Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin) are NO LONGER recommended due to widespread resistance. Always check current CDC guidelines.

Syphilis

Stage Timing Signs & Symptoms
Primary Syphilis 10-90 days post-exposure (avg 21 days) • Painless chancre (indurated ulcer) at inoculation site
• Usually single, round, firm lesion
• Genital, anal, or oral location
• Non-tender regional lymphadenopathy
• Heals spontaneously in 3-6 weeks without treatment
Secondary Syphilis 4-10 weeks after chancre appears • Diffuse maculopapular rash (including palms and soles - classic)
• Condyloma lata (flat, moist wart-like lesions in warm moist areas)
• Mucous patches in mouth
• Patchy alopecia
• Fever, malaise, generalized lymphadenopathy
• Highly contagious stage
Latent Syphilis After secondary symptoms resolve • Early latent: <1 year duration, asymptomatic
• Late latent: >1 year duration, asymptomatic
• No visible symptoms but infection persists
• Can last years to decades
Tertiary Syphilis 10-30 years after initial infection • Gummas (granulomatous lesions in organs, bones, skin)
• Cardiovascular: aortitis, aortic aneurysm, aortic regurgitation
• Neurosyphilis: tabes dorsalis, general paresis, meningitis, stroke
• Can be fatal
Category Details
Diagnostic Tests Non-treponemal tests (screening):
• RPR (Rapid Plasma Reagin)
• VDRL (Venereal Disease Research Laboratory)
• Detect antibodies; titers decrease with treatment
• Can have false positives (pregnancy, autoimmune disease)

Treponemal tests (confirmatory):
• FTA-ABS (Fluorescent Treponemal Antibody Absorption)
• TP-PA (Treponema pallidum Particle Agglutination)
• Remain positive for life even after treatment

Neurosyphilis: CSF analysis (VDRL-CSF)
Treatment Primary, Secondary, Early Latent:
• Benzathine penicillin G 2.4 million units IM single dose

Late Latent, Tertiary (not neurosyphilis):
• Benzathine penicillin G 2.4 million units IM weekly x 3 weeks

Neurosyphilis:
• Aqueous crystalline penicillin G 18-24 million units IV daily x 10-14 days

Penicillin Allergy: Desensitization required for pregnant women; doxycycline for non-pregnant
Jarisch-Herxheimer Reaction • Acute febrile reaction within 24 hours of treatment
• Caused by release of toxins from dying bacteria
• Symptoms: fever, chills, headache, myalgia, tachycardia
• Self-limiting (resolves in 24 hours)
• Treat symptomatically with antipyretics
• NOT an allergic reaction - can continue penicillin
Nursing Considerations • All pregnant women should be screened at first prenatal visit
• High-risk women: rescreen in 3rd trimester and at delivery
• Congenital syphilis can cause stillbirth, neonatal death, severe disabilities
• Treat all sexual partners
• Follow-up serologic testing at 6, 12, and 24 months
• Report to public health department
• Test for other STIs, including HIV
Clinical Pearl: Syphilis "Great Imitator" Syphilis is called the "great imitator" because it can mimic many other diseases. The classic rash on palms and soles in secondary syphilis is highly characteristic. Remember: painless chancre (primary) vs. painful ulcers (herpes). Always screen pregnant women - congenital syphilis is preventable with treatment.

Viral STIs

Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV-1 and HSV-2)

Category Details
Organism HSV-1: Primarily oral herpes (can cause genital)
HSV-2: Primarily genital herpes (can cause oral)
Both are DNA viruses
Transmission • Direct contact with lesions or secretions
• Can transmit when asymptomatic (viral shedding)
• Oral-genital contact can transmit HSV-1 to genitals
• Vertical transmission during childbirth
Primary Infection • Painful vesicular lesions (blisters) → ulcers → crusting
• Multiple grouped vesicles on erythematous base
• Severe pain, itching, burning
• Dysuria (if urethra involved)
• Tender inguinal lymphadenopathy
• Systemic symptoms: fever, headache, malaise, myalgia
• Lasts 2-3 weeks
Recurrent Infection • Virus remains dormant in nerve ganglia
• Triggers: stress, illness, immunosuppression, menstruation, sun exposure
• Prodrome: tingling, burning, itching before lesions appear
• Fewer lesions, less severe, shorter duration (5-10 days)
• No systemic symptoms typically
• Frequency decreases over time
Diagnostic Tests • Viral culture from lesion (must be active lesion)
• PCR (most sensitive) - preferred for CNS infection
• Type-specific HSV serology (IgG antibodies)
• Tzanck smear (less common) - shows multinucleated giant cells
Treatment NO CURE - treatment suppresses virus

Primary Episode:
• Acyclovir 400 mg PO TID x 7-10 days
• Valacyclovir 1 g PO BID x 7-10 days
• Famciclovir 250 mg PO TID x 7-10 days

Recurrent Episodes:
• Same medications, shorter duration (5 days)
• Start at first sign of prodrome

Suppressive Therapy (frequent recurrences):
• Daily antiviral medication
• Reduces outbreaks by 70-80%
• Reduces transmission risk
Complications • Neonatal herpes (can be fatal) - especially with primary infection during delivery
• Aseptic meningitis
• Urinary retention (autonomic neuropathy)
• Increased HIV transmission risk
• Eczema herpeticum in patients with atopic dermatitis
• Herpes keratitis (eye infection - can cause blindness)
Nursing Considerations • Counsel about chronic, incurable nature
• Abstain from sex during outbreaks (including prodrome)
• Condom use reduces but doesn't eliminate transmission
• Disclosure to partners recommended
• Suppressive therapy for pregnant women starting at 36 weeks
• C-section if active lesions at time of delivery
• Encourage support groups for emotional support
• Keep lesions clean and dry
Key Point: Herpes vs. Syphilis
  • Herpes: Painful vesicles/ulcers, recurrent, incurable (antivirals suppress)
  • Syphilis: Painless chancre (primary), curable with penicillin, can progress to tertiary

Human Papillomavirus (HPV)

Category Details
Organism Human papillomavirus (>100 strains); DNA virus
Types Low-risk types (6, 11): Cause genital warts (condyloma acuminata)
High-risk types (16, 18, 31, 33, 45, others): Cause cervical, anal, oropharyngeal cancers
• HPV 16/18 cause ~70% of cervical cancers
• HPV 6/11 cause ~90% of genital warts
Most Common STI Most common viral STI; most sexually active people get HPV at some point
Signs & Symptoms Genital Warts (low-risk HPV):
• Soft, flesh-colored or gray papules
• Can be flat, raised, or cauliflower-like
• Usually painless (may itch)
• Anogenital region: vulva, vagina, cervix, penis, scrotum, anus
• Size varies from pinpoint to large masses

High-risk HPV: Usually asymptomatic until cancer develops
Diagnostic Tests • Clinical diagnosis for visible warts
• HPV DNA testing (high-risk types) - used with Pap smear in women ≥30
• Pap smear (cervical cytology) - screens for cervical changes
• Colposcopy with biopsy if abnormal Pap
• Biopsy if diagnosis uncertain
Treatment Genital Warts (no cure for virus, treat lesions):
Provider-applied:
• Cryotherapy (liquid nitrogen) - most common
• TCA (trichloroacetic acid)
• Surgical removal, electrocautery, laser

Patient-applied:
• Imiquimod 5% cream (immune response modifier)
• Podofilox 0.5% solution
• Sinecatechins 15% ointment

High-risk HPV: No specific antiviral; treat precancerous/cancerous lesions
• Many infections clear spontaneously within 1-2 years
HPV Vaccine Gardasil 9: Protects against HPV 6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58
• Recommended ages 11-12 (can start at age 9)
• Catch-up vaccination through age 26
• Adults 27-45: shared clinical decision-making
• 2-dose series (ages 9-14) or 3-dose series (≥15 years)
• Given before sexual activity begins (most effective)
• Also approved for males - prevents genital warts and anal/throat cancers
Complications • Cervical cancer
• Vulvar, vaginal, penile, anal cancers
• Oropharyngeal cancer
• Recurrent respiratory papillomatosis (rare, from vertical transmission)
• Psychological distress
Nursing Considerations • Educate about vaccine importance
• Regular cervical cancer screening (Pap smears)
• Warts may recur after treatment
• Condoms reduce but don't eliminate transmission
• Partner treatment not necessary unless warts present
• No test of cure
• Many HPV infections clear on their own
NCLEX Priority: HPV Vaccination HPV vaccination is a primary prevention strategy. Educate parents and adolescents about vaccine benefits. The vaccine is most effective when given before sexual debut. It prevents not only genital warts but also life-threatening cancers. Both males and females should be vaccinated.

HIV/AIDS

Category Details
Organism Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) - RNA retrovirus
Pathophysiology • Attacks CD4+ T-lymphocytes (helper T cells)
• Destroys immune system over time
• AIDS diagnosis: CD4 count <200 cells/mm³ OR AIDS-defining illness
• Normal CD4 count: 500-1,500 cells/mm³
Transmission • Sexual contact (vaginal, anal, oral)
• Blood exposure (sharing needles, transfusions, occupational)
• Vertical transmission (mother to child: pregnancy, delivery, breastfeeding)
• NOT transmitted by casual contact, saliva, tears, sweat
Stages of HIV Acute HIV Infection (2-4 weeks post-exposure):
• Flu-like symptoms: fever, fatigue, rash, sore throat, lymphadenopathy
• High viral load (very infectious)
• May be asymptomatic

Chronic/Clinical Latency (can last years):
• Asymptomatic or mild symptoms
• Virus replicating at low levels
• Can transmit virus
• With ART, may never progress to AIDS

AIDS (Advanced HIV):
• CD4 <200 or AIDS-defining illness
• Opportunistic infections: PCP, Candidiasis, CMV, TB, Toxoplasmosis
• Opportunistic cancers: Kaposi's sarcoma, lymphomas
• Without treatment, survival ~3 years
Diagnostic Tests Screening:
• HIV-1/2 antigen/antibody combination immunoassay (4th generation)
• Detects HIV as early as 2-4 weeks post-exposure

Confirmatory:
• HIV-1/HIV-2 antibody differentiation immunoassay
• HIV-1 RNA (viral load) if acute infection suspected

Monitoring:
• CD4 count (immune function)
• Viral load (amount of virus in blood)
• Goal: undetectable viral load with ART

Window Period: Time between infection and detectable antibodies (~3-12 weeks)
Treatment Antiretroviral Therapy (ART):
• Combination therapy (3+ drugs from ≥2 classes)
• Started immediately upon diagnosis
• Suppresses viral replication
• Must be taken daily for life
• Adherence critical (>95%) to prevent resistance

Drug Classes:
• NRTIs (nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors)
• NNRTIs (non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors)
• PIs (protease inhibitors)
• INSTIs (integrase strand transfer inhibitors) - preferred
• Entry/fusion inhibitors

Goals:
• Undetectable viral load (U=U: Undetectable = Untransmittable)
• Increase/preserve CD4 count
• Prevent opportunistic infections
• Improve quality of life and life expectancy
Prevention PrEP (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis):
• Tenofovir/emtricitabine (Truvada) or Descovy
• Daily pill for high-risk individuals
• 99% effective if taken as prescribed
• Requires HIV testing before starting

PEP (Post-Exposure Prophylaxis):
• Started within 72 hours of exposure (ideally within 2 hours)
• 28-day course of ART
• For occupational or sexual exposure

Other:
• Condom use, needle exchange programs
• Treatment as prevention (ART for HIV+ individuals)
• Routine screening
Nursing Considerations • Strict confidentiality
• Assess medication adherence
• Monitor for opportunistic infections
• Pregnant women: ART reduces vertical transmission to <1%
• No breastfeeding if HIV positive in developed countries
• Report to public health department
• Partner notification and testing
• Psychosocial support, reduce stigma
• Standard precautions in healthcare settings
• Teach about U=U (undetectable = untransmittable)
Clinical Pearl: U=U (Undetectable = Untransmittable) When a person with HIV maintains an undetectable viral load through consistent ART, they cannot sexually transmit HIV to partners. This is a critical concept for reducing stigma and encouraging treatment adherence. Educate patients that adherence to ART not only improves their health but also prevents transmission.

Hepatitis B

Category Details
Organism Hepatitis B virus (HBV) - DNA virus
Transmission • Sexual contact (semen, vaginal secretions)
• Blood exposure (needles, transfusions)
• Vertical transmission (mother to child at birth)
• Percutaneous/mucosal exposure to infected blood
• More infectious than HIV
Signs & Symptoms Acute Infection (many asymptomatic):
• Jaundice (yellowing of skin/sclera)
• Dark urine, clay-colored stools
• Fatigue, malaise, weakness
• Anorexia, nausea, vomiting
• Right upper quadrant pain
• Fever, headache, myalgia
• Symptoms last weeks to months

Chronic Infection (develops in 5-10% of adults):
• Often asymptomatic for years
• Can progress to cirrhosis, liver failure, hepatocellular carcinoma
• Higher risk if infected as infant (90% become chronic)
Diagnostic Tests HBsAg (Hepatitis B surface antigen):
• Positive = current infection (acute or chronic)

Anti-HBs (Hepatitis B surface antibody):
• Positive = immunity (from infection or vaccine)

Anti-HBc (Hepatitis B core antibody):
• IgM anti-HBc = acute infection
• IgG anti-HBc = past or chronic infection

HBeAg (Hepatitis B e antigen):
• Positive = high infectivity, active viral replication

HBV DNA: Measures viral load

Liver function tests: Elevated ALT, AST, bilirubin
Treatment Acute Infection:
• Supportive care (most recover without treatment)
• Rest, adequate nutrition, hydration
• Avoid alcohol and hepatotoxic drugs

Chronic Infection:
• Antivirals: tenofovir, entecavir, others
• Interferon alfa (less commonly used)
• Regular monitoring for liver damage and cancer
• Liver transplant if liver failure

Prevention:
• Hepatitis B vaccine (3-dose series)
• HBIG (Hepatitis B immune globulin) for post-exposure prophylaxis
Vaccination • Routine childhood vaccination (birth, 1-2 months, 6-18 months)
• All unvaccinated adults at risk
• Healthcare workers
• Provides long-term immunity
• Check antibody titers if immunocompromised
Nursing Considerations • Screen all pregnant women
• Newborns of HBsAg+ mothers: give HBIG and vaccine within 12 hours of birth
• Report to public health department
• Educate about prevention: vaccination, safer sex, no needle sharing
• Monitor liver function in chronic cases
• Avoid alcohol (hepatotoxic)
• Screen for hepatocellular carcinoma (chronic HBV)
• Standard precautions in healthcare
NCLEX Alert: Hepatitis B Vaccination All healthcare workers should be vaccinated against Hepatitis B. Newborns receive their first dose within 24 hours of birth. If a mother is HBsAg positive, the newborn must receive BOTH HBIG and the vaccine within 12 hours of birth. This is critical for preventing vertical transmission.

Other Important STIs

Trichomoniasis

Category Details
Organism Trichomonas vaginalis (flagellated protozoan parasite)
Characteristics Most common curable STI worldwide; only affects urogenital tract
Signs & Symptoms Women (70% asymptomatic):
• Profuse, frothy, yellow-green vaginal discharge with foul odor
• Vulvovaginal irritation and itching
• Dysuria, urinary frequency
• Dyspareunia (painful intercourse)
• "Strawberry cervix" (petechiae on cervix) - classic but only in 2%

Men (most asymptomatic):
• Urethral discharge
• Dysuria
• Urethral irritation
Diagnostic Tests • NAAT (nucleic acid amplification test) - most sensitive
• Wet mount microscopy: motile trichomonads (flagellated organisms)
• pH >4.5 (normal vaginal pH is 3.8-4.5)
• Whiff test may be positive (fishy odor with KOH)
Treatment Recommended:
• Metronidazole 2 g PO single dose OR
• Tinidazole 2 g PO single dose OR
• Metronidazole 500 mg PO BID x 7 days

Pregnancy: Metronidazole 2 g PO single dose (safe after first trimester)

• Treat all sexual partners simultaneously
• Avoid alcohol during and 24 hours after metronidazole (72 hours for tinidazole) - disulfiram reaction
Complications • Increased risk of HIV acquisition and transmission
• Pelvic inflammatory disease
• Pregnancy complications: preterm birth, low birth weight
• Increased susceptibility to other STIs
Nursing Considerations • Treat sexual partners (even if asymptomatic)
• Abstain from sex until both partners treated
• Avoid alcohol with metronidazole (disulfiram-like reaction)
• Test for other STIs
• Retest in 3 months (high reinfection rate)
Key Point: Trichomoniasis Remember the classic "strawberry cervix" and frothy, yellow-green, foul-smelling discharge. Trichomoniasis increases HIV transmission risk. Always treat partners simultaneously to prevent reinfection. Warn patients about the disulfiram reaction with alcohol and metronidazole.

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

Category Details
Definition Infection of upper genital tract: endometritis, salpingitis, tubo-ovarian abscess, pelvic peritonitis
Causative Organisms • Most commonly: Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae
• Also: anaerobes, Mycoplasma genitalium, enteric gram-negatives
• Often polymicrobial
Risk Factors • History of STI (especially untreated chlamydia/gonorrhea)
• Multiple sexual partners
• Age <25 years
• Recent IUD insertion (within 3 weeks)
• Douching
• Previous PID
Signs & Symptoms • Lower abdominal pain (bilateral)
• Abnormal vaginal discharge
• Fever >38.3°C (101°F)
• Cervical motion tenderness ("chandelier sign")
• Adnexal tenderness
• Uterine tenderness
• Abnormal uterine bleeding
• Dyspareunia
• May have nausea, vomiting
Diagnostic Tests • Clinical diagnosis (low threshold to treat)
• NAAT for gonorrhea and chlamydia
• Pregnancy test (rule out ectopic)
• Elevated WBC, ESR, CRP
• Pelvic ultrasound (if mass or abscess suspected)
• Laparoscopy (definitive diagnosis but rarely needed)
Diagnostic Criteria Minimum criteria (start empiric treatment):
• Sexually active woman
• Pelvic/lower abdominal pain
• Cervical motion tenderness OR uterine tenderness OR adnexal tenderness
• No other cause identified
Treatment Outpatient Treatment:
• Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose PLUS
• Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID x 14 days PLUS
• Metronidazole 500 mg PO BID x 14 days (for anaerobic coverage)

Inpatient Treatment (IV antibiotics) indicated if:
• Pregnancy
• Tubo-ovarian abscess
• Severe illness, high fever
• Unable to tolerate oral medications
• Failed outpatient treatment
• Surgical emergency cannot be ruled out
Complications • Infertility (12-18% after one episode, increases with each episode)
• Ectopic pregnancy (10x increased risk)
• Chronic pelvic pain
• Tubo-ovarian abscess
• Fitz-Hugh-Curtis syndrome (perihepatitis)
• Increased risk of recurrent PID
Nursing Considerations • Low threshold to treat - sequelae are serious
• Treat sexual partners
• Abstain from sex during treatment
• Remove IUD if present (controversial, case-by-case)
• Follow-up in 48-72 hours to ensure improvement
• Educate about long-term complications
• Encourage STI screening and prevention
NCLEX Alert: PID Treatment PID is a clinical diagnosis with a LOW threshold to treat because complications (infertility, ectopic pregnancy) are severe. The classic "chandelier sign" (cervical motion tenderness so severe the patient reaches for the chandelier) is highly suggestive. Always treat empirically if PID is suspected - don't wait for test results.

Bacterial Vaginosis (BV)

Category Details
Definition Polymicrobial syndrome; overgrowth of anaerobic bacteria replacing normal lactobacilli
Causative Organisms • Gardnerella vaginalis (most common)
• Prevotella, Bacteroides, Mobiluncus
• NOT considered an STI (but sexually associated)
• Disruption of normal vaginal flora
Signs & Symptoms • Thin, gray-white, homogeneous vaginal discharge
• Fishy odor (especially after intercourse or menses)
• Minimal to no irritation or itching
• 50% asymptomatic
Diagnostic Tests Amsel Criteria (3 of 4 needed):
1. Thin, gray-white discharge
2. pH >4.5 (normal vaginal pH 3.8-4.5)
3. Positive whiff test (fishy odor with 10% KOH)
4. Clue cells on wet mount (epithelial cells covered with bacteria)

• NAAT testing available
• Gram stain (Nugent score)
Treatment Recommended:
• Metronidazole 500 mg PO BID x 7 days OR
• Metronidazole gel 0.75% intravaginally daily x 5 days OR
• Clindamycin cream 2% intravaginally at bedtime x 7 days

Alternative:
• Clindamycin 300 mg PO BID x 7 days

• Treat symptomatic women and pregnant women
• Partner treatment NOT recommended (not an STI)
Complications • Increased susceptibility to STIs (including HIV)
• PID
• Pregnancy complications: preterm birth, preterm labor, chorioamnionitis, postpartum endometritis
• Post-surgical infections (hysterectomy, abortion)
Nursing Considerations • Partner treatment not necessary
• Avoid douching (disrupts vaginal flora)
• Avoid alcohol with metronidazole
• High recurrence rate (30% within 3 months)
• Screen and treat pregnant women (reduces pregnancy complications)
• Oil-based vaginal medications may weaken latex condoms
Key Point: BV vs. Trichomoniasis vs. Candidiasis
  • BV: Thin gray-white discharge, fishy odor, pH >4.5, clue cells, treat with metronidazole
  • Trichomoniasis: Frothy yellow-green discharge, foul odor, pH >4.5, motile trichomonads, treat with metronidazole
  • Candidiasis: Thick white cottage cheese discharge, itching, pH normal (4-4.5), pseudohyphae, treat with antifungals

STI Screening Guidelines

Population Screening Recommendations
Women <25 years • Annual screening for chlamydia and gonorrhea if sexually active
• HIV screening at least once
Women ≥25 years • Screen for chlamydia and gonorrhea if at increased risk
• Risk factors: new/multiple partners, partner with STI, inconsistent condom use
Pregnant Women • First prenatal visit: HIV, syphilis, hepatitis B, chlamydia, gonorrhea
• Third trimester (high risk): rescreen for HIV, syphilis, chlamydia, gonorrhea
• At delivery (if not screened): HIV, syphilis
Men Who Have Sex with Men (MSM) • Annual screening: HIV, syphilis, chlamydia, gonorrhea (urethral, rectal, pharyngeal sites)
• More frequent (every 3-6 months) if multiple partners or anonymous partners
• Hepatitis B vaccination if not immune
• Hepatitis C screening
All Sexually Active Individuals • HIV screening at least once (ages 13-64)
• Annual screening if high risk
• Screen for STIs based on risk factors and exposures
People with HIV • Screen for other STIs at initial evaluation
• At least annual screening for syphilis, gonorrhea, chlamydia
• More frequent if multiple partners

Reportable STIs

Mandatory Reporting to Public Health The following STIs must be reported to local/state health departments:
  • Chlamydia
  • Gonorrhea
  • Syphilis (all stages)
  • HIV/AIDS
  • Hepatitis B (acute and chronic)
  • Chancroid (rare in US)
Reporting helps track disease trends, identify outbreaks, and ensure partner notification. Confidentiality is maintained.

Patient Education & Nursing Interventions

Safe Sex Practices

  • Consistent condom use: Latex condoms reduce transmission of most STIs
  • Limit number of partners: Fewer partners = lower risk
  • Mutual monogamy: Both partners tested and in exclusive relationship
  • Communication: Discuss STI testing and sexual history with partners
  • Regular screening: Routine testing even if asymptomatic
  • Vaccination: HPV (Gardasil 9), Hepatitis B vaccines
  • Avoid douching: Disrupts normal vaginal flora

Medication Adherence

  • Complete full course: Take all medications even if symptoms resolve
  • Single-dose therapy: Observe patient taking medication if compliance concern
  • Partner treatment: Simultaneous treatment prevents reinfection
  • Abstinence during treatment: No sexual activity until treatment completed and symptoms resolved
  • Follow-up testing: Test of cure for certain infections, rescreen in 3 months

Special Considerations for Pregnant Women

  • Routine screening: All pregnant women screened for HIV, syphilis, hepatitis B, chlamydia
  • Treatment modifications: Some medications contraindicated (e.g., doxycycline)
  • Vertical transmission prevention: ART for HIV, penicillin for syphilis, C-section for active herpes
  • Neonatal prophylaxis: Erythromycin eye ointment (gonorrhea), HBIG and vaccine (hepatitis B)
  • Breastfeeding considerations: HIV-positive mothers should not breastfeed in developed countries

Nursing Assessment

  • Sexual history: Number of partners, types of sexual activity, condom use, previous STIs
  • Symptom assessment: Discharge, dysuria, lesions, pain, fever
  • Physical examination: Inspect genitals, lymph nodes, skin; pelvic exam for women
  • Psychosocial assessment: Emotional response, partner notification concerns, stigma
  • Risk assessment: Identify high-risk behaviors, barriers to prevention

Psychosocial Support

  • Non-judgmental care: Avoid stigmatizing language, maintain professional demeanor
  • Confidentiality: Strict privacy protection, discuss only with healthcare team
  • Emotional support: Acknowledge distress, provide counseling referrals
  • Partner notification: Discuss strategies, offer public health assistance
  • Support groups: Connect with others (especially for chronic infections like herpes, HIV)

NCLEX Practice Questions

Question 1: A 22-year-old woman is diagnosed with chlamydia. Which medication should the nurse anticipate being prescribed?
A) Penicillin G benzathine 2.4 million units IM
B) Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID for 7 days
C) Acyclovir 400 mg PO TID for 7-10 days
D) Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose
Answer: B) Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID for 7 days
Rationale: Doxycycline is the first-line treatment for chlamydia. Azithromycin 1g PO single dose is an alternative. Penicillin treats syphilis, acyclovir treats herpes, and ceftriaxone treats gonorrhea.
Question 2: A nurse is teaching a patient diagnosed with genital herpes. Which statement by the patient indicates understanding?
A) "I should take antibiotics whenever I have an outbreak."
B) "This infection can be cured with antiviral medications."
C) "I can transmit the virus even when I don't have symptoms."
D) "I should stop taking my medication once the blisters heal."
Answer: C) "I can transmit the virus even when I don't have symptoms."
Rationale: Herpes can be transmitted through asymptomatic viral shedding. Herpes is a viral infection (antivirals, not antibiotics) that is incurable and requires lifelong management. Suppressive therapy continues daily even without symptoms.
Question 3: A patient presents with a painless ulcer on the genitals. The nurse suspects primary syphilis. Which diagnostic test confirms this diagnosis?
A) NAAT for Chlamydia trachomatis
B) RPR and confirmatory FTA-ABS
C) Viral culture of the lesion
D) Wet mount microscopy
Answer: B) RPR and confirmatory FTA-ABS
Rationale: Syphilis is diagnosed with non-treponemal tests (RPR or VDRL) for screening and treponemal tests (FTA-ABS or TP-PA) for confirmation. The painless chancre is characteristic of primary syphilis.
Question 4: Which patient teaching is most important for a patient taking metronidazole for trichomoniasis?
A) "Take this medication with food to reduce nausea."
B) "Avoid alcohol while taking this medication and for 24 hours after."
C) "You may experience photosensitivity, so wear sunscreen."
D) "Sexual partners do not need treatment if asymptomatic."
Answer: B) "Avoid alcohol while taking this medication and for 24 hours after."
Rationale: Metronidazole causes a disulfiram-like reaction with alcohol (nausea, vomiting, headache, flushing). All sexual partners should be treated simultaneously, even if asymptomatic, to prevent reinfection.
Question 5: A nurse is assessing a patient with suspected pelvic inflammatory disease (PID). Which finding is most characteristic?
A) Painless vaginal bleeding
B) Cervical motion tenderness
C) Thick, white, cottage cheese-like discharge
D) Vesicular lesions on the vulva
Answer: B) Cervical motion tenderness
Rationale: Cervical motion tenderness (chandelier sign) is a hallmark of PID, along with uterine and adnexal tenderness. This finding, combined with lower abdominal pain in a sexually active woman, indicates empiric treatment should be started.
Question 6: A pregnant woman at 28 weeks gestation tests positive for syphilis. What is the priority nursing action?
A) Schedule a cesarean section delivery
B) Administer benzathine penicillin G as prescribed
C) Advise the patient to stop breastfeeding
D) Start antiviral suppressive therapy
Answer: B) Administer benzathine penicillin G as prescribed
Rationale: Penicillin is the only effective treatment for preventing congenital syphilis. Treatment during pregnancy can prevent vertical transmission. Cesarean section is not indicated for syphilis. Antivirals treat herpes, not syphilis.

Key NCLEX Takeaways

Top 10 NCLEX Nursing Priorities for STIs
  1. Chlamydia is the most common bacterial STI and often asymptomatic - screen all sexually active women <25 annually
  2. Gonorrhea treatment is ceftriaxone IM only - fluoroquinolones no longer used due to resistance
  3. Syphilis stages: Painless chancre (primary) → rash on palms/soles (secondary) → asymptomatic (latent) → organ damage (tertiary)
  4. Herpes is incurable - antivirals suppress but don't eliminate virus; can transmit when asymptomatic
  5. HPV vaccine prevents cancer - recommended ages 11-12, most effective before sexual debut
  6. HIV undetectable = untransmittable (U=U) - ART adherence prevents transmission
  7. PID has low threshold to treat - cervical motion tenderness + lower abdominal pain = start treatment empirically
  8. Pregnant women need comprehensive STI screening - test for HIV, syphilis, hep B, chlamydia, gonorrhea
  9. Partner treatment is essential - simultaneous treatment prevents ping-pong reinfection
  10. Reportable diseases: Chlamydia, gonorrhea, syphilis, HIV, hepatitis B must be reported to public health
Clinical Pearl: Memory Aids
  • Painful vs. Painless Ulcers: Herpes = "Hurts" (painful); Syphilis = "Silent" (painless chancre)
  • Discharge Colors: Chlamydia/Gonorrhea = yellow-green; Trichomoniasis = frothy yellow-green with odor; BV = gray-white with fishy odor; Candida = white cottage cheese
  • Metronidazole = NO ALCOHOL: Think "Metro" (metronidazole) = "Don't drive drunk" (avoid alcohol - disulfiram reaction)
  • Doxycycline Contraindications: Pregnancy, children <8 years (teeth staining), photosensitivity
  • PID "Chandelier Sign": Cervical motion tenderness so severe patient "reaches for the chandelier"
Final Key Point: Prevention is Key The best treatment for STIs is prevention through education, screening, vaccination, and safer sex practices. As nurses, we play a crucial role in reducing stigma, encouraging testing, ensuring treatment adherence, and preventing transmission. Approach all patients with compassion, confidentiality, and evidence-based care.
