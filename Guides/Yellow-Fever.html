🦠 Section 1 — Etiology & Transmission

Yellow Fever Virus

  • Agent: Yellow fever virus — a single-stranded RNA virus of the family Flaviviridae, genus Flavivirus
  • Related viruses: Dengue, Zika, West Nile, Japanese encephalitis — all flaviviruses transmitted by mosquitoes
  • Reservoir: Primates (monkeys) in jungle/sylvatic cycle; humans in urban cycle
  • Vector: Aedes aegypti mosquito (urban cycle) — same mosquito that transmits dengue, Zika, and chikungunya
  • Haemagogus and Sabethes mosquitoes in the jungle/sylvatic cycle (South America)

Transmission Cycles

CycleVectorHostsSetting
Sylvatic (Jungle)Haemagogus, Aedes africanusMonkeys → humans (incidental)Tropical forest. Workers entering jungle (loggers, farmers).
Intermediate (Savanna)Aedes spp.Monkeys ↔ humansSemi-domestic mosquitoes. Small outbreaks in African villages near forests.
UrbanAedes aegyptiHumans → humansCities and towns. Large epidemics. Most dangerous from public health perspective.
⚡ Key Fact: Yellow fever is NOT transmitted person-to-person through casual contact, respiratory droplets, or body fluids. The mosquito vector is REQUIRED for transmission.

Epidemiology

  • Endemic areas: Tropical regions of Africa (90% of cases) and South America
  • NOT present in Asia — despite Aedes aegypti abundance (reasons debated)
  • Estimated 200,000 cases/year worldwide (WHO); 30,000–60,000 deaths/year
  • Incubation period: 3–6 days after mosquito bite
  • Period of viremia (infectious to mosquitoes): approximately 3–5 days after symptom onset
🔬 Section 2 — Pathophysiology & Clinical Phases

Pathophysiology

  • Virus enters via mosquito bite → replicates in regional lymph nodes → viremia → targets the liver (primary target organ), kidneys, heart, and immune system
  • Liver damage: Councilman bodies (eosinophilic necrosis of hepatocytes) — pathognomonic finding on liver biopsy. Mid-zone necrosis pattern (spares portal areas).
  • Kidney damage: Acute tubular necrosis → oliguria → renal failure
  • Coagulopathy: ↓ clotting factor synthesis (liver failure) + DIC → hemorrhage
  • Jaundice: From hepatocellular damage — gives the disease its name ("yellow")

Clinical Phases

PhaseTimingSymptomsKey Features
1. Infection (Acute/Viremic)Days 1–3Sudden fever (39–40°C), headache, myalgia, nausea, photophobia, facial flushingViremia is present. Patient is infectious to mosquitoes. Resembles many other febrile illnesses.
2. RemissionDays 3–4Fever breaks, symptoms improveLasts 24–48 hours. ~85% of patients recover here. Does NOT mean the patient is cured — 15% progress to toxic phase.
3. Toxic Phase (Intoxication)Days 4–7+High fever returns, jaundice, hemorrhage, hematemesis ("black vomit"), oliguria, delirium, seizuresMulti-organ failure. Faget sign (fever with relative bradycardia). Case fatality rate: 20–50% in toxic phase.
🔴 Faget Sign — Pathognomonic Clinical Finding:
  • Definition: Fever with paradoxical bradycardia (pulse-temperature dissociation)
  • Normal response: HR increases ~10 bpm for each 1°F of fever
  • Faget sign: Temperature > 102°F (39°C) but HR remains < 100 bpm or even decreases
  • Cause: Direct viral myocarditis affecting the SA node
  • Also seen in: Typhoid fever, Legionnaires' disease, drug fever, beta-blocker use — but classically associated with yellow fever
⚡ NCLEX Rule: The remission phase is DECEPTIVE. A patient who initially improves may deteriorate rapidly into the toxic phase. Close monitoring must continue through the remission period.

"Black Vomit" — Hematemesis

Coffee-ground or black vomitus (hematemesis) during the toxic phase is a classic and ominous sign of yellow fever. It results from GI hemorrhage secondary to coagulopathy and liver failure. The term "vómito negro" (black vomit) was historically used to describe yellow fever outbreaks.

💊 Section 4 — Treatment & Management

Treatment — Supportive Care Only

There is NO specific antiviral treatment for yellow fever. Management is entirely supportive, focused on symptom management and preventing complications.

ComplicationManagement
Dehydration & feverIV fluid resuscitation. Acetaminophen for fever/pain. AVOID aspirin and NSAIDs (↑ bleeding risk).
HemorrhageBlood products: FFP for coagulopathy, platelets if thrombocytopenic, PRBCs for significant blood loss. Vitamin K if indicated.
Hepatic failureMonitor LFTs, INR, ammonia. Low-protein diet if encephalopathy. Lactulose for hepatic encephalopathy. Avoid hepatotoxic drugs.
Renal failureAggressive IV hydration. Monitor I&O, BUN/Cr, electrolytes. Dialysis if indicated.
DICTreat underlying cause. Blood products as needed. Monitor coagulation studies.
Secondary infectionBroad-spectrum antibiotics for bacterial superinfection. Culture-guided therapy.
Seizures / EncephalopathyBenzodiazepines for acute seizures. Monitor neurological status. Correct metabolic derangements.
🔴 NEVER Give These to Yellow Fever Patients:
  • Aspirin — ↑ bleeding (antiplatelet effect + Reye syndrome risk)
  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) — ↑ GI bleeding risk, ↓ platelet function
  • IM injections — ↑ hematoma risk in coagulopathic patients
  • Hepatotoxic drugs — acetaminophen caution (limit dose if hepatic impairment; use cautiously)

ICU-Level Monitoring

  • Continuous cardiac monitoring (Faget sign, dysrhythmias)
  • Strict I&O with hourly urine output monitoring (target > 0.5 mL/kg/hr)
  • Serial labs: CBC, CMP, LFTs, coagulation panel, lactate
  • Neurological assessment: Glasgow Coma Scale, pupil response, orientation
  • Hemorrhage monitoring: stool guaiac, NG aspirate, skin assessment for petechiae/ecchymosis
🩺 Section 5 — Nursing Care & Infection Prevention

Nursing Priorities

PriorityInterventions
1. Fluid balanceIV access (large bore). Fluid resuscitation per provider order. Strict I&O. Daily weights. Monitor for fluid overload vs. dehydration.
2. Hemorrhage surveillanceAssess for bleeding: gums, nose, injection sites, GI tract (hematemesis, melena, hematochezia). Avoid unnecessary venipunctures. Apply pressure to all puncture sites × 5+ minutes.
3. Neuro monitoringLevel of consciousness, orientation, seizure precautions, GCS assessment. Report acute changes immediately.
4. Mosquito protectionBed net over patient during viremic period (first 5 days of illness). Eliminate standing water. Screen windows. DEET-based repellent on exposed skin.
5. Liver-protective careLow-protein diet if hepatic encephalopathy risk. Avoid hepatotoxic drugs. Monitor ammonia levels. Assess for asterixis (liver flap).
6. Renal supportMonitor urine output hourly. Report output < 30 mL/hr. Adjust IV fluids. Prepare for possible dialysis. Monitor K⁺ (hyperkalemia risk in renal failure).
🟡 Infection Control for Yellow Fever:
  • Standard precautions for blood/body fluid contact
  • NOT airborne, droplet, or direct contact transmission — mosquito vector required
  • Blood precautions: careful handling of blood samples (virus present in blood during viremia)
  • Mosquito prevention is the key infection control measure: bed nets, screens, DEET repellent, eliminate breeding sites
  • No isolation room required (unlike TB or measles) — but protect from mosquitoes during viremic phase to prevent urban transmission

Patient & Family Teaching

  • Explain the disease course and the deceptive remission phase
  • Importance of mosquito avoidance even after feeling better (prevent transmission)
  • Signs of worsening: jaundice, dark urine, bleeding from any site, decreased urine output, confusion
  • After recovery: lifelong immunity develops (reinfection does not occur)
  • Emotional support: toxic phase is frightening — provide honest, compassionate communication
💉 Section 6 — Vaccination

Yellow Fever Vaccine (17D)

FeatureDetails
TypeLive attenuated virus vaccine (strain 17D)
DoseSingle SubQ injection
Onset of protection10 days after vaccination (antibodies develop)
DurationLifelong immunity (WHO position since 2013 — single dose is sufficient)
AdministrationOnly at authorized Yellow Fever Vaccination Centers (stamped on International Certificate of Vaccination)
International CertificateValid 10 days after vaccination. Required for entry to many countries in endemic zones.
Efficacy> 99% effective. One of the most successful vaccines ever developed.

Contraindications

🔴 Do NOT Vaccinate (Live Vaccine Contraindications):
  • Infants < 6 months — absolute contraindication (risk of encephalitis)
  • Infants 6–8 months — avoid unless high-risk exposure
  • Immunocompromised: HIV with CD4 < 200, active cancer therapy, high-dose corticosteroids, organ transplant recipients
  • Thymus disorders: History of thymectomy or thymoma
  • Severe egg allergy: Vaccine is grown in chicken embryo cells (skin testing available)
  • Pregnancy — theoretical risk to fetus (defer if possible; risk-benefit discussion if travel unavoidable)
  • Age > 60 years — ↑ risk of vaccine-associated viscerotropic disease (first-time vaccination; benefits vs. risks assessment)

Vaccine Adverse Events

ReactionIncidenceDescription
Mild~25%Low-grade fever, headache, myalgia, injection site soreness — days 5–10 post-vaccine. Self-limited.
YEL-AND (Neurotropic disease)~1 in 125,000Encephalitis, Guillain-Barré, ADEM. Higher risk in infants < 6 months and elderly > 60.
YEL-AVD (Viscerotropic disease)~1 in 250,000Multi-organ failure resembling wild-type yellow fever. Mortality ~60%. Higher risk in elderly, thymus disease.
Anaphylaxis~1 in 131,000Immediate hypersensitivity (egg protein, gelatin). Treat with epinephrine.
⚡ NCLEX Rule: Yellow fever vaccine is a LIVE vaccine. Never give to immunocompromised patients. Administer at least 4 weeks before travel (10 days for immunity + safety margin). Lifelong immunity from one dose.
🌍 Section 7 — Global Health & Prevention

WHO Eliminate Yellow Fever Epidemics (EYE) Strategy

  • Goal: Eliminate yellow fever epidemics by 2026
  • Three pillars:
    1. Protect at-risk populations (mass vaccination campaigns)
    2. Prevent international spread (vaccination requirements for travelers, stockpile management)
    3. Contain outbreaks rapidly (surveillance, rapid response, reactive vaccination)
  • Global vaccine stockpile of 6 million doses maintained for emergency response

Vector Control — Community-Level Prevention

StrategyImplementation
Eliminate breeding sitesRemove standing water (tires, flower pots, containers, gutters). Cover water storage. Weekly inspection.
Personal protectionDEET-based repellents. Long sleeves/pants during peak mosquito activity (dawn/dusk). Permethrin-treated clothing.
Physical barriersWindow/door screens. Bed nets (insecticide-treated). Air conditioning (reduces mosquito exposure).
Insecticide applicationIndoor residual spraying. Ultra-low volume (ULV) fogging during outbreaks. Larvicides in water containers.
SurveillanceSentinel surveillance sites. Rapid case reporting. Epi-linked outbreak investigation within 48 hours.

Travel Health Nursing

  • Assess travel itinerary for yellow fever risk zones
  • Ensure vaccination ≥ 10 days before departure
  • Issue International Certificate of Vaccination or Prophylaxis (ICVP)
  • Provide mosquito avoidance education
  • Advise to seek care if febrile illness develops within 2 weeks of returning from endemic area
  • Report suspected cases to public health authorities

Common NCLEX Yellow Fever Traps

  • Trap: "Place the patient in airborne isolation" — Yellow fever is NOT airborne. Mosquito-borne. Standard precautions + mosquito protection.
  • Trap: "Administer ibuprofen for fever" — NEVER. Use acetaminophen only. NSAIDs ↑ bleeding.
  • Trap: "The patient is improving (remission phase), discharge planning" — No! 15% of patients progress to lethal toxic phase. Continue close monitoring.
  • Trap: "Administer the yellow fever vaccine to the HIV+ patient with CD4 of 150" — Contraindicated. Live vaccine + immunocompromised = dangerous.
  • Trap: "Start antiviral therapy" — No specific antiviral exists for yellow fever. Treatment is supportive only.
