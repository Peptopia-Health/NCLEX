🫁 Section 1 — ABCs & Maslow's Framework

Airway → Breathing → Circulation (ABC)

The ABC hierarchy is the universal foundation of nursing priority. When multiple patients need attention, always address the patient with the highest-level compromise first:

A — Airway → B — Breathing → C — Circulation → D — Disability → E — Exposure
  • Airway obstruction always takes priority — without a patent airway, nothing else matters. Think: stridor, choking, anaphylactic tongue edema, post-op laryngospasm.
  • Breathing problems come next — SpO₂ < 90%, severe dyspnea, absent breath sounds, tension pneumothorax.
  • Circulation — active hemorrhage, systolic BP < 90 mmHg, pulseless extremity, signs of shock.
  • Disability — acute change in level of consciousness (GCS drop ≥ 2 points), unequal pupils, new focal neuro deficit.
  • Exposure — full body assessment, temperature regulation, environmental safety.
⚡ NCLEX Rule: If an answer choice addresses AIRWAY, it is almost always the correct priority — unless the patient is already stable and another patient has an acute, life-threatening change.

Maslow's Hierarchy Applied to Nursing

Maslow's hierarchy maps directly to nursing priority. Always address lower-level needs before higher-level needs:

LevelNeedNursing Example
1 — PhysiologicalOxygen, fluids, nutrition, elimination, painSuctioning airway, starting IV fluids, managing hypoglycemia (BG < 70 mg/dL)
2 — SafetyPhysical safety, infection control, fall preventionBed alarm, side rails up, seizure precautions, isolation
3 — Love/BelongingSocial support, family involvementFacilitating family visits, support groups
4 — EsteemDignity, autonomy, self-worthEncouraging independence in ADLs, respecting preferences
5 — Self-ActualizationGrowth, education, health promotionDischarge teaching, wellness education
⚡ NCLEX Tip: Physiological needs (oxygenation, circulation, pain) always beat psychosocial needs. Teaching and emotional support come AFTER physical stability.

Assess Before You Act

The nursing process starts with Assessment. On NCLEX, if "assess" or "check" is an answer option, it is often correct — the nurse should gather data before intervening.

Exceptions — Act IMMEDIATELY without assessment:
  • Cardiac/respiratory arrest → start CPR immediately
  • Complete airway obstruction → abdominal thrusts (Heimlich)
  • Active hemorrhage → apply direct pressure
  • Anaphylaxis with known trigger → administer epinephrine
  • Witnessed ventricular fibrillation → defibrillate
⚖️ Section 2 — Who to See First

The 4 Priority Principles

When the NCLEX asks "Which patient should the nurse see FIRST?", apply these rules in order:

#PrincipleSee FirstSee Later
1Unexpected over ExpectedPost-op patient with sudden BP 78/50Heart failure patient with chronic peripheral edema
2Unstable over StablePatient with O₂ sat 82% on room airCOPD patient with baseline SpO₂ 91% on 2L NC
3Acute over ChronicNew-onset chest painChronic angina managed with nitroglycerin PRN
4Change over BaselinePatient with new confusion (was alert × 4)Dementia patient at baseline orientation
⚡ The golden question: "Which patient is MOST LIKELY to deteriorate right now?" — that is who you see first.

Common "See First" NCLEX Scenarios

🔴 See IMMEDIATELY (life-threatening):
  • Chest pain with ST elevation or new dysrhythmia
  • Post-thyroidectomy stridor (airway compromise)
  • Acute change in level of consciousness
  • Post-cardiac cath with pulseless extremity
  • Blood transfusion reaction (fever, chills, flank pain)
  • Potassium > 6.0 mEq/L with ECG changes
  • Sickle cell patient with O₂ sat < 90% and chest pain (acute chest syndrome)
🟡 See Soon (potentially unstable):
  • New-onset confusion in an elderly patient (may indicate UTI, hypoxia, or stroke)
  • Post-op patient who hasn't voided in 8 hours
  • Diabetic patient with BG 52 mg/dL who is alert and able to swallow
  • Patient with new-onset peripheral numbness and tingling
  • Fever of 101.5°F (38.6°C) in a neutropenic patient
🟢 Can Wait (stable/expected):
  • Chronic heart failure patient with 2+ pitting edema (baseline finding)
  • Post-op day 2 patient requesting pain medication
  • Patient asking for discharge teaching
  • Stable patient requesting diet change

👥 Section 3 — Delegation & Scope of Practice

Scope of Practice — Who Can Do What

🩺 RN (Registered Nurse)

  • Assessment & reassessment
  • Nursing diagnosis
  • Care plan development
  • Patient/family teaching
  • Evaluation of outcomes
  • IV push medications
  • Blood product administration
  • Unstable patient care
  • New admission assessment
  • Discharge planning
  • Central line care
  • Telephone orders (receive & verify)

💉 LPN/LVN

  • Stable, predictable patients
  • Data collection (not initial assessment)
  • Oral & IM medications
  • Subcutaneous injections (including insulin)
  • Dressing changes (non-complex)
  • Foley catheter insertion & care
  • NG tube feedings
  • Suctioning (oral/tracheal)
  • Vital signs & documentation
  • Reinforce teaching (NOT initiate)
  • Wound care per protocol
  • IV monitoring (NOT initiation in most states)

🤝 UAP (Unlicensed Assistive Personnel)

  • ADLs: bathing, grooming, dressing
  • Vital signs (stable patients)
  • Ambulation & positioning
  • Intake & output measurement
  • Feeding (stable, no swallowing issues)
  • Weight measurement
  • Specimen collection
  • Hygiene & comfort measures
  • Bed-making & room preparation
  • Transport
  • Post-mortem care
  • CPR (certified)

The 5 Rights of Delegation

RightQuestion to AskExample
1. Right TaskCan this task be delegated?Vital signs → YES. Assessment → NO.
2. Right CircumstanceIs the patient stable enough?Stable post-op vitals → OK. Actively bleeding → NOT OK.
3. Right PersonIs this person competent for this task?Experienced UAP for ambulation; new UAP needs supervision.
4. Right DirectionDid I give clear, specific instructions?"Take vitals q4h; report SBP < 100 or > 160 immediately."
5. Right SupervisionAm I following up on the delegated task?RN checks back to ensure vitals were done and reviews results.

NEVER Delegate (The ATEN Rule)

🧠 ATEN — Never Delegate These
Assessment (initial & ongoing) · Teaching (initial education) · Evaluation (of outcomes) · Nursing judgment (clinical decisions)
Additional items that cannot be delegated:
  • Care of unstable patients (to LPN or UAP)
  • New admission assessments
  • Blood product administration (RN only)
  • IV push medications (RN only)
  • Patient education (LPN can reinforce, not initiate)
  • Discharge teaching
  • Calling the healthcare provider with assessment findings

Charge Nurse Role

The charge nurse's primary responsibility is coordinating care and making assignments. On NCLEX:

  • The charge nurse assigns the most complex patients to the most experienced RN.
  • Assign stable, predictable patients to the LPN/LVN.
  • Assign routine ADLs and vital signs to the UAP.
  • New graduate nurses should get stable patients with predictable outcomes.
  • Float/agency nurses should get patients that don't require specialized unit knowledge.

Disaster Triage (START Triage — Mass Casualty)

In a mass casualty incident (MCI), the priority shifts from "save the individual" to "save the MOST people." Resources are allocated to patients most likely to survive with treatment.

🔴 RED — Immediate Life-threatening but SURVIVABLE with treatment. Treat first.
🟡 YELLOW — Delayed Serious but can wait 30–60 min without deterioration.
🟢 GREEN — Minor Walking wounded. Minor injuries. Can self-treat or wait.
⚫ BLACK — Expectant Injuries incompatible with survival. Comfort care only.
⚡ CRITICAL NCLEX Distinction: In emergency triage, the most critical patient is treated first. In disaster triage, the patient who will benefit MOST from immediate treatment (RED tag) is treated first — NOT the most critically injured (BLACK tag).

🔥 Section 5 — Emergency Response Protocols

RACE — Fire Response

R — Rescue → A — Alarm → C — Contain → E — Extinguish / Evacuate
  • Rescue — Remove patients from immediate danger FIRST
  • Alarm — Pull the fire alarm and call the fire code
  • Contain — Close all doors and windows to limit fire spread
  • Extinguish — Use a fire extinguisher if safe to do so, or evacuate the area

PASS — Fire Extinguisher

P — Pull pin → A — Aim at base → S — Squeeze handle → S — Sweep side to side
⚡ NCLEX Rule: RESCUE is ALWAYS the first action in a fire. Remove patients first, THEN pull the alarm. The nurse's first priority is patient safety.

Code Blue — Cardiac Arrest Response

StepActionKey Detail
1Recognize & call for helpCheck responsiveness, call Code Blue / activate rapid response
2Start CPR30:2 ratio, compress 2–2.4 inches deep, rate 100–120/min
3DefibrillateVF/pulseless VT → shock ASAP; < 3 minutes ideal
4MedicationsEpinephrine 1 mg IV q3-5 min; Amiodarone 300 mg IV for refractory VF/VT
5Advanced airwayIntubation; continuous compressions once placed

Rapid Response Team (RRT) — Activation Triggers

Call the Rapid Response Team when a patient shows acute deterioration:

  • Respiratory: RR < 8 or > 28/min, SpO₂ < 90% despite O₂, new stridor
  • Cardiac: HR < 40 or > 130/min, SBP < 90 mmHg, new chest pain
  • Neurological: Acute change in mental status, new seizure, sudden focal weakness
  • Other: Urine output < 50 mL in 4 hours, staff member has significant concern

Chain of Command

If a nursing concern is not addressed by the primary provider, the nurse is obligated to escalate:

Primary Provider → Charge Nurse → Supervisor / Manager → Chief Medical Officer

The nurse is a patient advocate — never abandon a concern because one person dismisses it.

📞 Section 6 — SBAR & Communication

SBAR Framework

SBAR is the standardized communication tool used for nurse-to-provider handoffs and critical reports:

S Situation What is happening RIGHT NOW?
B Background What is the relevant clinical history?
A Assessment What do I think the problem is?
R Recommendation What do I think we should do?

SBAR Example — Nurse Calling Provider

S: "Dr. Smith, I'm calling about Mrs. Jones in Room 412. She's having new-onset chest pain rated 8/10 and is diaphoretic."
B: "She's a 68-year-old admitted for pneumonia. History of CAD, prior MI 2019. Currently on heparin drip and ceftriaxone."
A: "I'm concerned this may be an acute cardiac event. Her BP is 92/58, HR 112, SpO₂ 94%. ECG shows new ST elevation in leads II, III, aVF."
R: "I'd like to administer nitroglycerin 0.4 mg SL, obtain a troponin level, and have the patient evaluated at bedside."

Read-Back Protocol — Verbal & Telephone Orders

  1. Write it down — Document the order as it is being given.
  2. Read it back — Repeat the entire order to the provider verbatim.
  3. Receive confirmation — The provider must confirm "That's correct."

This applies to ALL verbal orders, telephone orders, and critical lab values.

💊 Section 7 — Time-Critical & Priority Medications

Medications That Change a Patient's Priority

When a patient has an order for one of these medications AND a matching clinical finding, that patient becomes a higher priority:

Time-Critical Medication Rules

  • First-dose antibiotics: Administer within 1 hour of order for suspected sepsis — draw blood cultures FIRST, then give the antibiotic.
  • Insulin: Give rapid-acting insulin with meals; do NOT hold for NPO without a provider order. Always check BG first.
  • Anticoagulants: Heparin drips require aPTT monitoring; Warfarin requires INR before administration.
  • Seizure medications: Never skip or delay phenytoin, levetiracetam, or valproic acid — risk of breakthrough seizures.
  • Thrombolytics: Strict time windows. Check for contraindications: recent surgery (< 14 days), active bleeding, BP > 185/110, history of hemorrhagic stroke.

Independent Double-Check Required

These high-alert medications require two nurses to independently verify before administration:

  • Insulin (all IV drips)
  • Heparin (IV drips and SQ high-dose)
  • Blood products (verify at bedside with 2 RNs)
  • Chemotherapy agents
  • Opioid PCA pumps
  • Potassium chloride (KCl) IV — NEVER give IV push; always diluted, always on a pump
  • Pediatric weight-based dosing calculations
🧠 Section 8 — Memory Anchors & Decision Trees

Priority Decision Algorithm

Use this quick mental checklist when facing any "see first" or "do first" NCLEX question:

Step 1: Is there an AIRWAY problem? → Address FIRST, always.
Step 2: Is there active BLEEDING or hemodynamic instability? → Address next.
Step 3: Which finding is UNEXPECTED for this patient's condition? → That patient first.
Step 4: When all patients are stable — use Maslow: physiological → safety → psychosocial.

Common NCLEX Traps

  • Trap: "Notify the provider" is listed as an option → Often NOT the first action. Assess and stabilize first, THEN notify.
  • Trap: A psychological symptom looks urgent → Remember Maslow. Physical needs (pain, oxygenation) always come before emotional/psychosocial needs.
  • Trap: "Document the finding" is an answer → Documentation is important but NEVER the first action when a patient is in distress.
  • Trap: All four patients seem critical → Re-read carefully. Look for the one with an UNEXPECTED finding or CHANGE from baseline — that's your answer.
  • Trap: Delegate to LPN or UAP → If the patient is unstable, changing, or requires assessment — the RN must handle it directly.
