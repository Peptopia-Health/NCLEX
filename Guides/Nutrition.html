⚡ NCLEX Rule: Prealbumin is the BEST indicator for acute/recent nutritional changes. Albumin is best for chronic/long-term status. Both are affected by inflammation and hydration.

Signs of Malnutrition

  • Unintentional weight loss > 10% in 6 months
  • Muscle wasting, fatigue, poor wound healing
  • Dry, flaky skin; brittle hair and nails; angular cheilitis (cracking at mouth corners)
  • Edema (in severe protein deficiency — kwashiorkor)
  • Weakened immune response (frequent infections)

🔑 Fat-Soluble Vitamins — "ADEK in the FAT"
Vitamins A, D, E, K — stored in fat. Can become toxic. Need bile for absorption.

🟡 Warfarin & Vitamin K Consistency:
  • Patients on warfarin should maintain CONSISTENT vitamin K intake — not avoid it entirely
  • Sudden ↑ in vitamin K–rich foods (kale, spinach, broccoli) → ↓ INR → ↑ clotting risk
  • Sudden ↓ in vitamin K–rich foods → ↑ INR → ↑ bleeding risk
  • Key message: "Keep your intake consistent day to day"
🔧 Section 5 — Enteral Nutrition (Tube Feeding)

Types of Feeding Tubes

TubeLocationDurationKey Points
NG (Nasogastric)Nose → StomachShort-term (≤ 4 weeks)Verify placement: X-ray (gold standard on initial placement), pH of aspirate (gastric ≤ 5.5), CO₂ detector. NEVER use auscultation alone.
NJ/ND (Nasoduodenal/Jejunal)Nose → Duodenum/JejunumShort-termPost-pyloric. Used when aspiration risk is high. pH > 6 (intestinal).
G-tube (Gastrostomy/PEG)Abdominal wall → StomachLong-termSurgically or endoscopically placed. Site care: clean with soap and water, rotate tube daily, assess for leakage.
J-tube (Jejunostomy)Abdominal wall → JejunumLong-termContinuous feeding only (no bolus — small bowel can't handle volume). Less aspiration risk.

Tube Feeding Administration

  • HOB elevation: ≥ 30–45° during and for 30–60 min after feeding (aspiration prevention)
  • Residual check: Check gastric residual before each bolus or every 4–6 hours for continuous. Hold if > 250–500 mL (per policy). Return aspirate to stomach.
  • Flush: With 30 mL warm water before/after feeding and every 4 hours for continuous (prevents clogging)
  • Hang time: Open system formula — max 4–8 hours. Closed system — max 24–48 hours.
  • Diarrhea management: Assess rate (too fast?), temperature (should be room temp), contamination, medications (sorbitol-containing), osmolality
🔴 Tube Feeding Complications:
  • Aspiration: #1 complication. Maintain HOB elevation. Check placement. Monitor for cough, crackles, ↓ SpO₂.
  • Clogging: Flush regularly. Do NOT mix medications with formula. Crush meds and flush between each.
  • Diarrhea: Slow rate, check osmolality, rule out C. diff, assess medications.
  • Refeeding syndrome: In severely malnourished patients — monitor K⁺, Mg²⁺, PO₄ (all drop dangerously). Start feeds slowly.
  • Dumping syndrome: (post-pyloric feeds) — nausea, cramping, diarrhea, diaphoresis. Slow rate. Small, frequent feeds.
💉 Section 6 — Parenteral Nutrition (TPN)

Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN)

IV nutrition given when the GI tract is non-functional or cannot be used. Contains: dextrose, amino acids, lipids, electrolytes, vitamins, minerals, trace elements.

FeaturePPN (Peripheral)TPN (Central)
Dextrose concentration≤ 10%Up to 70%
OsmolarityLower (< 900 mOsm/L)Higher (requires central line)
DurationShort-term (< 2 weeks)Long-term (can be indefinite)
AccessPeripheral IVCentral venous catheter (PICC, subclavian, internal jugular)
Caloric densityLowerHigher — can meet full nutritional needs

TPN Nursing Management

  • Administration: Via infusion pump. Dedicated central line lumen (no piggybacking other meds).
  • Blood glucose monitoring: Every 4–6 hours initially (high dextrose → hyperglycemia). Insulin may be added to TPN or given separately.
  • Rate changes: NEVER abruptly stop TPN (risk of rebound hypoglycemia). If must stop, taper or infuse D10W at same rate.
  • Filter: Use 0.22-micron filter for dextrose/amino acids; 1.2-micron for lipids.
  • Lipid infusion: May run separately. Hang for max 12 hours. Monitor triglycerides.
  • Labs: Daily weights, I&O, BMP (electrolytes, glucose, BUN/Cr), liver function, triglycerides, prealbumin.
🔴 TPN Complications:
  • Hyperglycemia: Most common. Monitor BG. Administer insulin as ordered.
  • Infection/sepsis: Strict aseptic technique for line care. Change tubing per policy. Monitor for fever, ↑ WBC.
  • Air embolism: Clamp tubing when changing bags. Position patient in left Trendelenburg if suspected.
  • Refeeding syndrome: ↓ K⁺, ↓ Mg²⁺, ↓ PO₄ as malnourished cells take up nutrients. Start TPN slowly. Monitor electrolytes closely.
  • Fluid overload: Monitor I&O, daily weights, lung sounds, edema.
⚡ NCLEX Rule: If TPN runs out or must be stopped emergently, hang D10W at the same rate to prevent rebound hypoglycemia. NEVER stop TPN abruptly.
👶 Section 7 — Special Populations

Pregnancy Nutrition

  • Calorie increase: +340 kcal/day (2nd trimester), +452 kcal/day (3rd trimester)
  • Weight gain (normal BMI): 25–35 lbs total
  • Folic acid: 600 mcg/day (prevents neural tube defects — start before conception ideally)
  • Iron: 27 mg/day (double non-pregnant needs; blood volume ↑ 50%)
  • Calcium: 1000–1300 mg/day
  • DHA: Important for fetal brain development (fatty fish, supplements)
  • Avoid: Raw fish/meat, high-mercury fish (swordfish, shark, king mackerel), unpasteurized dairy, deli meats (listeria risk), alcohol, excess caffeine (< 200 mg/day), liver (excess vitamin A = teratogenic)

Pediatric Nutrition

AgeNutrition NeedsKey Points
0–6 monthsBreast milk or formula exclusivelyNo water, juice, or solids. Breast milk is ideal. Vitamin D supplement (400 IU/day) for breastfed infants.
6–12 monthsIntroduce solids (iron-fortified cereal first)Single-ingredient foods, one at a time (wait 3–5 days between new foods for allergy monitoring). No honey < 1 year (botulism risk).
1–2 yearsWhole milk (not skim), table foodsNo choking hazards: hot dogs, grapes (cut), popcorn, nuts, raw carrots. Limit juice to 4 oz/day.
Toddlers/Preschool3 meals + 2 snacksSmall portions. Don't force eating. Iron & calcium important for growth. Limit screen time during meals.

Older Adult Nutrition

  • ↓ caloric needs but ↑ nutrient density needed
  • ↑ protein (1.0–1.2 g/kg/day) to prevent sarcopenia
  • ↑ calcium (1200 mg/day) + vitamin D (800–1000 IU/day) for osteoporosis prevention
  • ↑ fiber + fluids for constipation prevention
  • B12 supplementation often needed (↓ intrinsic factor with age)
  • Screen for food insecurity, dentition problems, dysphagia, depression-related appetite loss

Diabetes Nutrition

  • Carb counting: 1 carb choice = 15 g carbohydrates
  • Consistent carbohydrate distribution across meals
  • Monitor BG before and 2 hours after meals
  • Hypoglycemia rule of 15: If BG < 70 mg/dL → give 15 g fast-acting carbs → recheck in 15 min → repeat if still < 70
  • Glycemic index awareness: low-GI foods provide steadier glucose control

Common NCLEX Nutrition Traps

  • Trap: "Albumin is the best indicator of nutritional status" — For ACUTE changes, prealbumin is better. Albumin reflects CHRONIC status.
  • Trap: "Auscultate to verify NG tube placement" — This is NO LONGER considered reliable. X-ray (initial) or pH testing (ongoing) is required.
  • Trap: "The patient on warfarin should avoid green vegetables" — WRONG. They should maintain CONSISTENT intake, not avoid vitamin K.
  • Trap: "Give orange juice to a patient with potassium of 6.2 mEq/L" — That's hyperkalemia! OJ is HIGH in potassium. They need potassium-lowering interventions.
  • Trap: "Start TPN at full rate immediately" — In malnourished patients, start slowly to prevent refeeding syndrome.
