🧬 Section 1 — Normal Aging Changes

System-by-System Age-Related Changes

SystemNormal Aging ChangeNursing Implications
Cardiovascular↓ cardiac output, ↑ peripheral vascular resistance, stiffened arteries, ↓ baroreceptor sensitivityOrthostatic hypotension risk. Rise slowly. Monitor BP in multiple positions. ↑ risk of isolated systolic HTN.
Respiratory↓ lung elasticity, ↓ vital capacity, weaker cough reflex, ↓ ciliary function↑ risk of pneumonia & atelectasis. Encourage incentive spirometry, deep breathing, flu/pneumonia vaccines.
Renal↓ GFR (~1% per year after 40), ↓ renal blood flow, ↓ concentrating abilityDrug accumulation risk (adjust renally cleared meds). Dehydration risk. Creatinine may appear normal despite ↓ GFR (less muscle mass).
GI↓ saliva, ↓ gastric acid, ↓ peristalsis, ↓ liver metabolismConstipation common. Drug metabolism slower. Encourage fiber, fluids, activity. Assess for malnutrition.
Musculoskeletal↓ bone density, ↓ muscle mass (sarcopenia), ↓ joint flexibility, ↓ height (vertebral compression)Fracture risk (especially hip). Weight-bearing exercise. Calcium + vitamin D. Fall prevention essential.
Neurological↓ reaction time, ↓ deep sleep, ↓ short-term memory, ↓ neurotransmittersAllow extra time for responses. Sleep pattern changes are NORMAL (not insomnia). Distinguish normal aging from pathology.
SensoryPresbyopia (near vision ↓), presbycusis (high-frequency hearing ↓), ↓ taste/smellLarger print. Speak clearly, lower pitch (NOT louder). Season food safely. Safety hazards (can't smell gas/smoke).
Integumentary↓ subcutaneous fat, ↓ elasticity, ↓ sweat glands, thinning skin, slower wound healingSkin tears easily. Pressure injury risk. Thermoregulation impaired (hypothermia risk). Avoid tape on fragile skin.
Immune↓ T-cell function, ↓ antibody response, chronic low-grade inflammation↑ infection risk. Fever response may be blunted — a temp of 99°F may indicate infection. Vaccines critical.

⚡ NCLEX Rule: Know the difference between NORMAL aging and PATHOLOGY. Confusion is NOT normal aging — it always warrants investigation (UTI, medications, dehydration, infection, stroke).

🔴 Delirium = Medical Emergency — Find and Treat the Cause:
  • UTI is the #1 cause of acute confusion in the elderly
  • Check medications (anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, opioids)
  • Assess for dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, constipation, pain
  • Rule out hypoxia, infection, MI, stroke
  • Maintain calm, well-lit environment. Reorient frequently. Avoid restraints.

Alzheimer's Disease — Stages

StageCharacteristicsNursing Care
Early (Mild)Short-term memory loss, word-finding difficulty, getting lost in familiar places, poor judgmentMaintain routine. Calendars, lists, labels. Safety assessment. Discuss advance directives while patient can participate.
Middle (Moderate)Wandering, sundowning, agitation, personality changes, inability to perform ADLs, incontinence beginsStructured routine. Redirection (not confrontation). Wandering precautions (ID bracelet, door alarms). Toileting schedule. Caregiver support.
Late (Severe)Non-verbal, bedbound, total dependence, dysphagia, loss of recognitionComfort care. Aspiration precautions. Skin care. Palliative approach. Family support and hospice referral.
⚡ NCLEX Rule: For agitated dementia patients — REDIRECT, don't argue or confront. Use validation therapy. Never say "Don't you remember?" or correct their reality. Meet them where they are.

Dementia Medications

  • Donepezil (Aricept) — Cholinesterase inhibitor. Slows cognitive decline (does not cure). Give at bedtime. Side effects: N/V, diarrhea, bradycardia.
  • Memantine (Namenda) — NMDA receptor antagonist. For moderate-to-severe Alzheimer's. Can be combined with donepezil. Side effects: dizziness, headache, confusion.

Sundowning

  • Increased confusion/agitation in late afternoon and evening
  • Interventions: Structured daily routine, adequate daytime lighting, limit caffeine, calm evening environment, night light, reduce stimulation before bed, assess for pain or hunger
⚠️ Section 3 — Fall Prevention & Safety

Fall Risk Factors in Older Adults

CategoryRisk Factors
IntrinsicAge > 65, history of falls, gait/balance impairment, visual/hearing deficits, orthostatic hypotension, cognitive impairment, urinary urgency/incontinence, arthritis, neuropathy, deconditioning
ExtrinsicWet floors, poor lighting, throw rugs, cluttered pathways, ill-fitting footwear, lack of grab bars, unfamiliar environment (hospital)
MedicationsSedatives/hypnotics, opioids, antihypertensives, diuretics, antihistamines, antipsychotics, benzodiazepines, antidepressants (any drug causing dizziness, sedation, or orthostatic hypotension)

Fall Prevention Interventions

  • Bed in lowest position with wheels locked
  • Call light within reach — answer promptly
  • Non-skid footwear (not socks alone)
  • Adequate lighting (night light in bathroom)
  • Bed alarm for high-risk patients
  • Two side rails up (all four = restraint)
  • Hourly rounding — assess needs (toileting, pain, positioning)
  • Assistive devices within reach (walker, cane, glasses, hearing aids)
  • Toileting schedule — don't make patients wait
  • Exercise programs to improve strength and balance (tai chi, PT referral)
🟡 Orthostatic Hypotension Prevention:
  • Dangle at bedside before standing (sit for 1–2 minutes)
  • Rise slowly from lying → sitting → standing
  • Check orthostatic BP: lying → sitting → standing (drop of SBP ≥ 20 or DBP ≥ 10 = positive)
  • Adequate hydration. Review medications contributing to orthostasis.

Hip Fracture — Common Geriatric Emergency

  • Signs: Shortened, externally rotated affected leg; pain in groin/hip; inability to bear weight
  • Pre-op: Buck's traction (skin traction) for comfort. Pain management. Pre-op teaching. Assess circulation distal to fracture.
  • Post-op (total hip replacement): Avoid hip flexion > 90°, adduction past midline, and internal rotation. Abduction pillow between legs. Elevated toilet seat. Avoid crossing legs.
  • Complications: DVT (prophylaxis with enoxaparin), PE, infection, fat embolism (long bone fractures)
💊 Section 4 — Polypharmacy & Medication Safety

Polypharmacy — The #1 Medication Risk in Elderly

Polypharmacy = taking 5+ medications concurrently. Older adults take more medications and are more vulnerable to adverse effects due to age-related pharmacokinetic changes.

Pharmacokinetic ChangeEffect in ElderlyClinical Impact
↓ Absorption↓ gastric acid, ↓ GI motility, ↓ blood flow to gutDelayed onset of oral meds. Some drugs may have ↓ absorption.
↑ Distribution↑ body fat, ↓ lean mass, ↓ total body water, ↓ albuminFat-soluble drugs (diazepam) have longer duration. ↓ albumin → more free drug → ↑ toxicity.
↓ Metabolism↓ liver blood flow, ↓ hepatic enzyme activityFirst-pass effect reduced → higher drug levels. Longer half-lives.
↓ Excretion↓ GFR, ↓ renal blood flowDrug accumulation. Adjust doses for CrCl/GFR. Monitor renally cleared drugs closely.

Beers Criteria — Potentially Inappropriate Medications in Elderly

🔴 HIGH-RISK Medications to AVOID in Older Adults:
  • Benzodiazepines (diazepam, alprazolam) — ↑ fall risk, cognitive impairment, paradoxical agitation
  • Anticholinergics (diphenhydramine, oxybutynin) — confusion, urinary retention, constipation, dry mouth, blurred vision
  • Meperidine (Demerol) — neurotoxic metabolite causes seizures
  • Long-acting sulfonylureas (glyburide) — prolonged hypoglycemia
  • Muscle relaxants (cyclobenzaprine) — anticholinergic effects, sedation
  • Non-COX selective NSAIDs (indomethacin) — GI bleeding, renal toxicity
  • First-generation antihistamines (diphenhydramine) — highly anticholinergic
  • Barbiturates — high dependence, tolerance, overdose risk
🚫 Anticholinergic Side Effects — "Can't see, can't pee, can't spit, can't sh*t"
Blurred vision · Urinary retention · Dry mouth · Constipation · Also: confusion, tachycardia, hyperthermia

Medication Reconciliation

  • Review ALL medications (prescription, OTC, herbal, supplements) at every transition of care
  • Use "brown bag" method — have patient bring ALL bottles/containers to appointment
  • "Start low, go slow" — begin at lowest dose and titrate gradually
  • Assess for drug-drug, drug-food, and drug-disease interactions
  • Simplify regimens where possible (once-daily dosing, combination pills)
  • Assess ability to open bottles, read labels, and self-administer correctly
⚡ NCLEX Rule: When an older adult develops NEW confusion, urinary retention, constipation, or falls — ALWAYS consider medications as a cause. Review the med list first.

Nutrition in the Older Adult

  • Caloric needs decrease with age (↓ metabolism, ↓ activity) but nutrient needs remain the same or increase
  • Protein needs increase slightly — 1.0–1.2 g/kg/day to prevent sarcopenia
  • Calcium: 1200 mg/day (women > 50, men > 70) for osteoporosis prevention
  • Vitamin D: 800–1000 IU/day (many elderly are deficient — ↓ sun exposure, ↓ skin synthesis)
  • Vitamin B12: Absorption decreases with age (↓ intrinsic factor). Screen for deficiency. Supplements may be needed.
  • Fiber: 25–30 g/day with adequate fluids to prevent constipation
  • Hydration: ↓ thirst sensation with age. Encourage fluids. Monitor for dehydration (poor skin turgor on forehead/sternum is more reliable than arm in elderly).
🟡 Malnutrition Warning Signs in Elderly:
  • Unintentional weight loss > 5% in 1 month or > 10% in 6 months
  • BMI < 18.5 or albumin < 3.5 g/dL or prealbumin < 15 mg/dL
  • Difficulty chewing (poor dentition), dysphagia, loss of appetite
  • Social isolation, depression, poverty, inability to shop/cook
  • Refer to dietitian, assess for Meals on Wheels or community resources
🩺 Section 6 — Pain Management & Skin Integrity

Pain Assessment in Older Adults

  • Pain is NOT a normal part of aging — always assess and treat
  • Older adults may underreport pain (fear of being a burden, belief that pain is expected)
  • Use validated tools: numeric (0–10), FACES scale, or behavioral tools (PAINAD) for cognitively impaired patients
  • Non-verbal cues: grimacing, guarding, restlessness, withdrawal, moaning, resisting care
  • Start low, go slow with analgesics. Monitor closely for adverse effects.
Pain Management in Elderly — Preferred Approach:
  • Acetaminophen — first-line for mild-moderate pain (max 2000–3000 mg/day in elderly)
  • Topical agents (lidocaine patches, diclofenac gel) — fewer systemic side effects
  • Non-pharmacological: heat/cold, massage, positioning, relaxation, distraction, PT
  • AVOID meperidine (seizures), long-acting opioids as first-line, and NSAIDs long-term (GI/renal risks)
  • If opioids needed: start at 25–50% of standard adult dose. Monitor respiratory status.

Pressure Injuries (Pressure Ulcers)

Pressure Injury Prevention

  • Reposition every 2 hours (use a turning schedule)
  • Keep skin clean and dry. Moisture barrier cream for incontinence.
  • Adequate nutrition (protein, vitamin C, zinc for wound healing)
  • Use pressure-redistribution surfaces (specialty mattresses)
  • Avoid shearing forces — lift, don't drag. Use draw sheets.
  • Float heels off the bed (pillow under calves, NOT under knees)
  • Braden Scale assessment on admission and regularly thereafter
🕊️ Section 7 — End-of-Life Care

Hospice & Palliative Care

FeaturePalliative CareHospice
EligibilityAny stage of serious illnessPrognosis of ≤ 6 months if disease runs its natural course
GoalSymptom management + curative treatment may continueComfort only. Curative treatment stops.
FocusQuality of life, symptom control, goals of careDignified death, comfort, family support
SettingHospital, clinic, homeHome, hospice facility, or inpatient unit

Signs of Approaching Death

  • Weeks before: ↓ appetite, ↑ sleeping, withdrawal from activities, ↓ interest in surroundings
  • Days before: Cheyne-Stokes breathing, mottled skin, cool extremities, ↓ urine output, terminal restlessness
  • Hours before: "Death rattle" (pooled secretions), Cheyne-Stokes → irregular breathing, cyanosis, unresponsive
End-of-Life Nursing Priorities:
  • Comfort above all: Pain management (do NOT withhold opioids due to respiratory depression — comfort is the goal)
  • Oral care: Mouth swabs, lip moisturizer (even when NPO)
  • Position: HOB elevated for dyspnea. Side-lying for secretions.
  • Emotional support: Allow family presence. Active listening. Respect cultural/spiritual practices.
  • Do NOT suction the "death rattle" (distressing to patient). Reposition and use glycopyrrolate or atropine drops.
  • Hearing is the last sense to go — always speak to the patient as if they can hear.

Grief & Bereavement

TypeDescription
Anticipatory griefGrieving begins before the actual loss (e.g., terminal diagnosis). Allow expression.
Normal griefSadness, crying, anger, guilt, sleep/appetite changes. Resolves over months.
Complicated griefProlonged, severe, interferes with daily function > 12 months. Requires professional support.
Disenfranchised griefLoss not openly acknowledged (e.g., miscarriage, pet loss, ex-spouse). Validate the loss.

Post-Mortem Care

  • Verify death with provider. Note time of death.
  • Follow facility policy for body preparation. Respect cultural/religious practices.
  • Allow family time with the body if desired
  • Remove tubes/lines per policy (unless medical examiner case — leave everything in place)
  • Can be delegated to UAP (supervised by RN)

Common NCLEX Gerontology Traps

  • Trap: "Confusion is expected in a 90-year-old" — WRONG. Acute confusion is always abnormal. Investigate the cause.
  • Trap: "Give diphenhydramine (Benadryl) for insomnia" — AVOID in elderly per Beers Criteria. Highly anticholinergic.
  • Trap: "Apply restraints for the wandering patient" — Restraints are LAST resort. Use redirection, companionship, wandering alarms first.
  • Trap: "Withhold opioids because RR is 14" — RR 14 is normal. Withhold at RR < 12. In end-of-life care, comfort is priority.
  • Trap: "Normal creatinine means normal kidney function" — In elderly, ↓ muscle mass means creatinine may be falsely normal despite ↓ GFR. Always check GFR/CrCl.
