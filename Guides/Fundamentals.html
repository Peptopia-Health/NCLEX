📋 Section 1 — The Nursing Process (ADPIE)

Five Steps of the Nursing Process

A — Assessment → D — Diagnosis → P — Planning → I — Implementation → E — Evaluation
StepWhat the Nurse DoesNCLEX Application
AssessmentCollect subjective & objective data. Health history, physical exam, vital signs, lab review.Assessment is almost always the FIRST correct action. "Assess" or "check" answers are usually right unless there's an emergency.
DiagnosisIdentify actual or potential nursing problems based on assessment data.Nursing diagnosis ≠ medical diagnosis. "Risk for..." identifies potential problems. "Impaired..." identifies actual problems.
PlanningSet SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Realistic, Time-bound). Establish priorities.Outcomes must be patient-centered and measurable. "Patient will ambulate 50 feet by discharge" — not "Nurse will encourage ambulation."
ImplementationCarry out the care plan. Direct care, teaching, medication administration, coordination.Independent nursing actions don't require a provider order. Dependent actions DO require an order.
EvaluationDetermine if goals were met. Reassess and modify the plan as needed.If the goal is NOT met → reassess, NOT re-implement the same intervention. Go back to Assessment.
⚡ NCLEX Rule: When in doubt, ASSESS FIRST. The only exceptions: CPR, choking (Heimlich), active hemorrhage (direct pressure), anaphylaxis (epinephrine), and witnessed VF (defibrillate).

Types of Assessment Data

  • Subjective data: What the patient TELLS you — "I feel dizzy," "My pain is 8/10," "I haven't slept in 3 days"
  • Objective data: What the nurse OBSERVES/MEASURES — BP 180/110, diaphoresis, wound drainage, SpO₂ 88%
  • Primary source: The patient themselves (most reliable)
  • Secondary sources: Family, medical records, other healthcare providers

Clinical Judgment — NCSBN Model

The NCLEX Next Generation (NGN) uses the Clinical Judgment Measurement Model (CJMM):

Recognize Cues → Analyze Cues → Prioritize Hypotheses → Generate Solutions → Take Action → Evaluate Outcomes
🛡️ Section 2 — Patient Safety & Risk Management

Fall Prevention

  • Risk factors: Age > 65, medications (sedatives, diuretics, opioids, antihypertensives), cognitive impairment, gait instability, history of falls, environmental hazards
  • Interventions: Bed in lowest position, call light within reach, bed alarm activated, non-skid footwear, adequate lighting, side rails up ×2 (not all four — that's restraint), hourly rounding
  • Post-fall: Assess patient FIRST (not the environment). Check for injury, neuro status, vital signs. Notify provider. Document and complete incident report.
⚡ NCLEX Rule: Assess the PATIENT first after a fall, not the environment. Even if the question asks "What should the nurse do FIRST?" — assess the patient's injury status.

Restraints — Use as LAST Resort

TypeOrder RenewalKey Requirements
Non-violent (medical)Every 24 hoursConfused patient pulling at lines/tubes. Try alternatives first (sitter, redirection, mittens).
Violent (behavioral)Adults: every 4 hours
Adolescents 9–17: every 2 hours
Children < 9: every 1 hour		Acute agitation/danger to self or others. Remove ASAP when patient is calm.
🔴 Restraint Safety Rules:
  • Check circulation, sensation, and movement (CMS) every 1–2 hours
  • Release restraints every 2 hours for ROM, toileting, nutrition, and repositioning
  • Tie knots with quick-release knots to a part of the bed frame that moves with the HOB
  • 1–2 fingers should fit between restraint and skin
  • NEVER tie to side rails. NEVER use as punishment. NEVER leave patient unmonitored.
  • Standing PRN order for restraints is NOT acceptable — must be individualized

Seizure Precautions

  • During seizure: Stay with patient. Time the seizure. Turn to side (if possible). Protect head (pad if needed). Do NOT restrain. Do NOT put anything in mouth. Suction and O₂ at bedside.
  • After seizure (postictal): Maintain airway (lateral position). Assess LOC, orientation, breathing. Perform neuro check. Document seizure type, duration, and post-ictal behavior.
  • Call for help if: Seizure lasts > 5 minutes (status epilepticus), repeated seizures without regaining consciousness, or injury occurs.

Fire Safety — RACE & PASS

R — Rescue → A — Alarm → C — Contain → E — Extinguish / Evacuate

Evacuation order: Ambulatory patients first → wheelchair patients → bed-bound patients. Move horizontally first (same floor), then vertically (downstairs). Use stairs, NEVER elevators.

Incident Reports

  • Complete for ANY unexpected event: falls, medication errors, near-misses, equipment malfunction, adverse reactions
  • Document what happened factually in the patient's chart — do NOT write "incident report filed" in the chart
  • The incident report is an internal quality improvement document — not part of the medical record
  • Report within 24 hours. No blame — focus on facts and system improvement.
🦠 Section 3 — Infection Control Essentials

Chain of Infection — Break Any Link to Prevent Spread

Infectious Agent → Reservoir → Portal of Exit → Mode of Transmission → Portal of Entry → Susceptible Host

Standard Precautions — For ALL Patients

  • Hand hygiene (most important single measure to prevent infection)
  • Gloves for contact with blood, body fluids, mucous membranes, non-intact skin
  • Gown if splash/spray risk or contact with contaminated surfaces
  • Mask + eye protection if splash/spray of blood/body fluids
  • Sharps disposal — never recap needles (activate safety mechanism, dispose immediately)
  • Respiratory hygiene/cough etiquette for anyone with respiratory symptoms

Transmission-Based Precautions

DropletSurgical mask within 3–6 feetPrivate room (no negative pressure needed)Influenza, pertussis, meningococcal meningitis, mumps, rubella, diphtheria, RSV, COVID-19

Surgical Asepsis (Sterile Technique)

🟢 Sterile Field Rules:
  • A 1-inch border around the sterile field is considered contaminated
  • Items below the waist are considered contaminated
  • If you turn your back on the sterile field → contaminated
  • If a sterile item gets wet (strike-through) → contaminated
  • When in doubt → it IS contaminated. Start over.
  • Open sterile packages: flap away from you first, then sides, then toward you last
  • Pour solutions from 4–6 inches above the container

Hand Hygiene

  • Alcohol-based rub: Preferred for routine hand hygiene (minimum 20 seconds). NOT effective against C. difficile spores or Norovirus.
  • Soap and water: Required when hands are visibly soiled, after caring for C. diff patient, and before/after eating.
  • When to perform: Before and after patient contact, before aseptic procedures, after body fluid exposure, after touching patient surroundings (WHO 5 Moments).
💬 Section 4 — Therapeutic Communication

Therapeutic Techniques

TechniqueExamplePurpose
Open-ended questions"Tell me about your pain."Encourages patient to elaborate. Gathers comprehensive data.
Reflection"You seem worried about the surgery."Mirrors patient's feelings. Shows empathy and understanding.
Restating/Paraphrasing"So you're saying the pain is worse at night?"Verifies understanding. Encourages clarification.
Silence(Allowing time to process)Gives patient time to think. Shows patience and respect.
Focusing"Let's talk more about your breathing difficulty."Directs conversation to priority concern.
Clarifying"What do you mean by 'feeling funny'?"Seeks specifics when information is vague.
Summarizing"Today we discussed your diet changes and new medication schedule."Reviews key points. Provides closure.
Offering self"I'll sit with you for a while."Shows presence and concern without conditions.
Validating"It's understandable to feel anxious before surgery."Acknowledges patient's feelings as normal and acceptable.

Non-Therapeutic Responses — AVOID These

🔴 Blocks to Communication:
  • False reassurance: "Don't worry, everything will be fine." — Dismisses concerns.
  • Giving advice: "I think you should…" — Takes away patient autonomy.
  • Changing the subject: Redirecting when the patient is expressing feelings.
  • Asking "Why?": "Why did you stop taking your medication?" — Sounds accusatory.
  • Agreeing/Disagreeing: "You're right, that doctor is terrible." — Not objective.
  • Using clichés: "God only gives you what you can handle." — Not helpful.
  • Defensive response: "The nurses here are excellent." — Dismisses patient's concern.
⚡ NCLEX Rule: On communication questions, ALWAYS choose the response that addresses the patient's FEELINGS first. If a patient says "I'm scared," the best response acknowledges the fear, not the facts.

Cultural Competence

  • Use professional interpreters (not family members) for non-English-speaking patients
  • Respect dietary preferences, religious practices, and health beliefs
  • Some cultures prefer same-sex caregivers — ask and accommodate
  • Eye contact norms vary — some cultures consider direct eye contact disrespectful
  • Ask about folk remedies or traditional medicine use — assess for interactions
📝 Section 5 — Documentation & Reporting

Documentation Principles

  • Timely: Document as close to the event as possible. Late entries should be noted as such.
  • Accurate: Use exact quotes for subjective data. Record objective measurements precisely.
  • Complete: Include assessment, intervention, and patient response.
  • Objective: Avoid opinions. Write "Patient crying, stated 'I feel hopeless'" — NOT "Patient is depressed."
  • Legal: The medical record is a legal document. If it wasn't documented, it wasn't done.
🟡 Documentation DON'Ts:
  • NEVER use whiteout, erasure, or write over errors — single line through, initial, date, time
  • NEVER leave blank spaces in documentation
  • NEVER chart for someone else or before care is given
  • NEVER document "incident report filed" in the patient chart
  • NEVER use unapproved abbreviations (refer to facility's "Do Not Use" list)

SBAR Communication

LetterComponentContent
SSituationWhat is happening RIGHT NOW? Why are you calling?
BBackgroundRelevant history, diagnosis, current treatment, allergies
AAssessmentWhat do you think the problem is? Current vitals, change from baseline
RRecommendationWhat do you want the provider to do? Specific order requests.

Telephone / Verbal Orders

  • Write down the order as it is given
  • Read back the order verbatim to the provider
  • Receive confirmation — "That's correct"
  • Provider must co-sign within the facility's required timeframe (typically 24 hours)

Change-of-Shift Report (Handoff)

  • Report at the bedside when possible (promotes patient involvement and safety)
  • Include: name, age, diagnosis, current status, recent changes, pending tests/procedures, IV/medications, dietary needs, safety concerns, code status
  • Do NOT include opinions, gossip, or judgmental statements
👥 Section 6 — Delegation & Leadership

Scope of Practice

🩺 RN Only

  • Initial assessment & reassessment
  • Nursing diagnosis & care planning
  • Patient/family teaching (initiation)
  • Evaluation of outcomes
  • IV push medications
  • Blood product administration
  • Unstable patient care
  • Telephone/verbal orders
  • Central line care
  • Discharge planning

💉 LPN/LVN Can Do

  • Stable, predictable patients
  • Data collection (not initial assessment)
  • Oral & IM medications
  • SubQ injections (insulin, heparin)
  • Dressing changes (non-complex)
  • Foley insertion & care
  • NG tube feedings
  • Suctioning
  • Reinforce teaching (NOT initiate)
  • Vital signs & documentation

🤝 UAP Can Do

  • ADLs: bathing, grooming, dressing
  • Vital signs (stable patients only)
  • Ambulation & positioning
  • Intake & output
  • Feeding (no swallowing issues)
  • Specimen collection
  • Weight measurement
  • Comfort measures
  • CPR (if certified)
  • Transport

Five Rights of Delegation

RightQuestion
Right TaskCan this legally be delegated? (Assessment, Teaching, Evaluation, Nursing judgment = NEVER delegate)
Right CircumstanceIs the patient stable? Is the situation appropriate? Is it within the person's scope?
Right PersonIs this person competent and trained for this specific task?
Right DirectionDid I give clear, specific instructions including parameters to report?
Right SupervisionAm I following up and evaluating the outcome?
🚫 ATEN — NEVER Delegate These
Assessment · Teaching · Evaluation · Nursing Judgment

Charge Nurse Assignments

  • Most complex patients → most experienced RN
  • Stable, predictable patients → LPN/LVN or new graduate
  • Routine ADLs → UAP
  • Float/agency nurses → patients that don't require specialized unit knowledge
  • Same-acuity patients → balanced distribution across staff
⚖️ Section 7 — Ethics & Legal Practice

Ethical Principles

PrincipleDefinitionNursing Example
AutonomyPatient's right to make their own decisionsRespecting a patient's refusal of treatment, even if you disagree. Informed consent.
BeneficenceDo good; act in patient's best interestProviding pain management. Advocating for patient needs.
NonmaleficenceDo no harmChecking allergies before administering medications. Safe medication practices.
JusticeFair & equal treatment for allTriage decisions based on acuity, not personal factors. Equal resource allocation.
FidelityFaithfulness; keeping promisesFollowing through on commitments. "I'll be back to check on you in 30 minutes."
VeracityTruth-tellingHonest disclosure about diagnosis, prognosis, and treatment options.

Informed Consent

  • Who obtains consent: The PROVIDER performing the procedure (not the nurse)
  • Nurse's role: Witness the signature. Verify patient understanding. Clarify questions. Advocate if patient seems confused or coerced.
  • Requirements: Patient must be competent, informed (risks, benefits, alternatives), and consent must be voluntary
  • Cannot consent: Minors (parent/guardian), unconscious patients (implied consent for emergencies), patients under influence of sedatives
  • Emancipated minors CAN consent: Married, in military, self-supporting, pregnant, or court-declared

Advance Directives & End-of-Life

  • Living will: Documents patient's wishes for end-of-life care (before incapacity)
  • Durable Power of Attorney for Healthcare: Designates a person to make healthcare decisions if patient cannot
  • DNR (Do Not Resuscitate): No CPR if patient stops breathing or heart stops. Does NOT mean "do not treat." Continue all other care.
  • Nurse's duty: Ask about advance directives on admission. Document. Follow them regardless of personal beliefs.

HIPAA & Confidentiality

HIPAA Key Rules:
  • Share patient information only on a need-to-know basis with the healthcare team
  • Do NOT discuss patients in public areas (elevators, cafeteria, hallways)
  • Avoid social media posts about patients — even without names (identifying details can violate HIPAA)
  • Log off computers when stepping away. Face screens away from visitors.
  • Patient can request to see their medical records and request corrections
  • Exceptions to confidentiality: Mandatory reporting (child/elder abuse, communicable diseases, gunshot wounds, threats of harm to self or others)

Mandatory Reporting

  • Suspected child abuse or neglect
  • Suspected elder abuse or neglect
  • Communicable/reportable diseases (TB, STIs, food-borne illness)
  • Gunshot and stab wounds
  • Impaired healthcare worker (substance abuse on duty)
  • Threats of harm to self (suicidal ideation) or others (Tarasoff duty to warn)

Common NCLEX Fundamentals Traps

  • Trap: "Notify the provider" — Often not the first action. Assess and stabilize first.
  • Trap: "Document the finding" — Documentation is important but NEVER the first action during a patient emergency.
  • Trap: The question asks about teaching → "Reinforce" is the LPN answer, "Initiate" is the RN answer. Know who is asking.
  • Trap: "Restrain the patient" for safety → Restraints are LAST resort. Try alternatives first (sitter, redirection, environmental modification).
  • Trap: A competent patient refuses treatment → You must respect autonomy even if you disagree. Document the refusal and educate on consequences.
