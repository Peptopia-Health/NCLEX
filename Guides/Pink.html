The Rule of the B's

pH & Bicarb go the same direction = Metabolic. Different directions = Respiratory.
LabNormal RangeNotes
pH7.35 – 7.45<7.35 = acidosis; >7.45 = alkalosis
HCO3 (Bicarb)22 – 26Metabolic marker
CO235 – 45Respiratory marker (inverse relationship)

"As the pH Goes, So Goes My Patient"

EXCEPT potassium — potassium is inverse to pH.

Alkalosis (pH Up)

  • Irritability
  • Hyper-reflexia (3+ & 4+)
  • Tachypnea
  • Tachycardia
  • Borborygmi (hyperactive bowels)
  • Seizure

Acidosis (pH Down)

  • Hypo-reflexia
  • Bradycardia
  • Lethargy
  • Paralytic ileus
  • Coma
  • Respiratory arrest
Kussmaul breathing = MAC — Metabolic ACidosis

Clinical Scenarios

  • Ask: "Is it a lung problem?" → Yes = Respiratory. No = Metabolic.
  • Over-ventilating = Respiratory Alkalosis
  • Under-ventilating = Respiratory Acidosis
  • Prolonged gastric vomiting / suctioning = Metabolic Alkalosis
  • Default guess (when unsure) = Metabolic Acidosis

Manipulation

Manipulation = dangerous / harmful acts. Differentiate from co-dependency behaviors.

Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome

  • B1 (Thiamine) deficiency
  • Amnesia with confabulation (making up stories) → Redirect
  • Preventable & arrestable, but ~70% of damage is irreversible

Antabuse

  • Antabuse (Disulfiram): Takes 2 weeks to enter body / 2 weeks to leave
  • Avoid ALL alcohol-containing products (cough syrup, mouthwash, aftershave, perfume)
  • CAN have red wine vinaigrette (vinegar, not alcohol)

Aminoglycosides

"A Mean Old Mycin" = serious gram-negative infections
  • Examples: Gentamicin, Tobramycin, Amikacin, Neomycin, Streptomycin
  • NOT mean old mycins (the “thro” = throw off the list): Erythromycin, Azithromycin, Clarithromycin

Toxicities

  • Ototoxic: hearing loss, tinnitus, vertigo (Cranial Nerve 8)
  • Nephrotoxic: monitor creatinine
The Number 8: Cranial nerve 8, administer every 8 hours, the 8 fits in a kidney shape
  • Route: IM or IV only
  • Trough: 30 min before dose (any route)
  • Peak: IV = 15–30 min after; IM = 30–60 min after

Calcium Channel Blockers

"Valium for the Heart" = negative inotropic, chronotropic, & dromotropic
  • Treat: HTN, angina, atrial arrhythmias, SVT
  • Drugs: Ends in -dipine (Amlodipine, Nifedipine) + Cardizem (Diltiazem) & Verapamil
  • Hold if: systolic BP < 100
  • Side effects: headache, hypotension

Cardiac Arrhythmias

Know 4 patterns: NSR, V-fib, V-tach, Asystole + A-fib & A-flutter

PVCs (Premature Ventricular Contractions)

  • Usually low priority
  • High priority if: >6/min, >6 in a row, or falls on T wave (R-on-T)

Electrolyte Rules

Kalemias (potassium) = SAME as prefix (hyper-K = everything hyper) EXCEPT HR & urine output
Calcemias (calcium) = OPPOSITE of prefix
Magnesiums = OPPOSITE of prefix
  • Chvostek sign: Cheek tap → facial spasm = LOW calcium
  • Trousseau sign: BP cuff → hand spasm = LOW calcium
  • Numbness/tingling = earliest sign of ANY electrolyte disorder

High Potassium Treatment

  • Fast/Temporary: D5W + Regular Insulin
  • Slow/Permanent: Kayexalate

Sodium

  • High sodium = Dehydration
  • Low sodium = Fluid overload

Hyperthyroidism = Hypermetabolism

  • Weight loss, high pulse/BP, heat intolerance, exophthalmos
  • Graves’ disease

Treatments:

  • Radioactive iodine: isolate 24 hrs, flush toilet 3×
  • PTU (Propylthiouracil): immunosuppressor, monitor WBCs
  • Thyroidectomy

Thyroid Storm = EMERGENCY

Signs: Temps >105°F, severe HTN, severe tachycardia, psychotic delirium
FIRST: Ice packs
BEST: Cooling blanket
Also: O2 10L via mask
NO Tylenol (acetaminophen)

Hypothyroidism = Hypometabolism

  • Obese, cold intolerance = Myxedema
  • Treatment: Synthroid (Levothyroxine)
  • NEVER sedate a hypothyroid patient
  • Never hold thyroid pills

Phenothiazines (1st Generation — end in -zine)

ABCDEFG:
Anticholinergic | Blurred vision | Constipation | Drowsiness | EPS (extrapyramidal symptoms) | Photosensitivity (F for “fried in the sun”) | Agranulocytosis

Examples: Chlorpromazine, Fluphenazine

Tricyclics

ABCDE: A through D (same as above) + Euphoria. Takes 2–4 weeks to work.

Examples: Elavil (Amitriptyline), Tofranil (Imipramine)

Benzodiazepines (end in -zep)

  • Minor tranquilizers
  • Uses: pre-op sedation, muscle relaxant, alcohol withdrawal, seizures
  • Max duration: 2–4 weeks

Examples: Diazepam, Lorazepam, Alprazolam

Lithium

3 P’s of Lithium: Peeing, Pooping, Paresthesia
  • Toxic signs: tremors, metallic taste, severe diarrhea
  • Linked to sodium — low Na = high Li toxicity

Prozac (SSRI)

  • Prozac (Fluoxetine): causes insomnia → give before noon
  • Watch for suicidal risk in adolescents with dose changes

Haldol

  • Haldol (Haloperidol): Only major antipsychotic safe in pregnancy
  • Available in decanoate (long-acting IM) form

NMS (Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome)

Life-threatening! Temps 106–108°F. Take temperature to distinguish NMS from EPS.

Special Drugs

  • Clozapine: No ABCDEF side effects BUT DOES have agranulocytosis (trashes bone marrow)
  • Geodon (Ziprasidone): BLACK BOX WARNING — prolongs QT interval, risk of sudden cardiac arrest

BAD Signs

  • Low variability
  • Late decelerations

OKAY Signs

  1. Deliver head

4 things, 4 times per hour:

  2. Fundus — Boggy = massage; Displaced = catheterize

Post-Op Care

  • Log roll
  • Don’t dangle legs
  • Don’t sit >30 minutes
  • MAY: walk, stand, lie down
Anterior thoracic approach = chest tube needed.

Fusion Surgery

  • 2 incisions: spine + hip (donor site)
  • Most pain at the hip
  • JP drain at the hip
  • Highest rejection risk at the spine

Restrictions

Permanent

  • Lift with knees
  • Cervical: never lift overhead

Temporary (6 weeks)

  • No sitting >30 min
  • Log roll
  • No driving
  • Nothing >5 lbs

4 Rules of Prioritization

  1. Acute > Chronic
  2. Fresh post-op > Medical
  3. Unstable > Stable
  4. More vital organ = tiebreaker
Organ Vitality Ranking: Brain > Lung > Heart > Liver > Kidney > Pancreas

The Modifying Phrase

The modifying phrase (descriptor) is what matters most — NOT the diagnosis, NOT the age/gender.

Unstable Words

  • Acute
  • <12 hr post-op
  • Lab Level C or D
  • Newly admitted/diagnosed
  • Changing assessments

Stable Words

  • Chronic
  • >12 hr post-op
  • Lab Level A or B
  • Ready for discharge
  • Unchanged assessments
ALWAYS unstable: Hemorrhage, Fever >105°F, Hypoglycemia, Pulselessness/Breathlessness

LPN Scope — CANNOT Do

  • Start an IV
  • Hang, mix, or push IV medications
  • Administer blood products
  • Central line care
  • Plan care / Develop teaching plans
  • Care for unstable patients
  • First of anything (first assessment, first dressing change, etc.)
  • Admission, discharge, or transfer assessments

UAP Scope — CANNOT Do

  • Chart
  • Give medications (EXCEPT topical OTC barrier creams)
  • Assess (EXCEPT vitals & accuchecks)
  • Treat (EXCEPT enemas)
