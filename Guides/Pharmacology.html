💊 Section 1 — Pharmacokinetics & Drug Safety

Pharmacokinetics — ADME

Pharmacokinetics describes what the body does to the drug. The four processes are Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, and Excretion:

ProcessDescriptionKey Nursing Implications
AbsorptionDrug moves from site of administration into bloodstreamOral drugs affected by food, pH, GI motility. IV = 100% bioavailability. IM faster than SubQ.
DistributionDrug travels via blood to target tissuesProtein-binding affects drug availability — low albumin → more free drug → increased toxicity risk.
MetabolismLiver (primarily) converts drug to metabolitesFirst-pass effect reduces oral drug availability. Hepatic impairment → drug accumulation. CYP450 interactions.
ExcretionDrug eliminated, primarily via kidneysMonitor BUN & creatinine for renal dosing. Elderly have ↓ GFR → slower excretion → ↑ toxicity risk.
⚡ NCLEX Rule: If a patient has hepatic or renal impairment, ALWAYS anticipate reduced drug clearance and need for dose adjustment.

Pharmacodynamics — Drug-Receptor Interactions

Pharmacodynamics describes what the drug does to the body:

  • Agonist — Binds receptor and activates it (e.g., morphine at opioid receptors)
  • Antagonist — Binds receptor and blocks it (e.g., naloxone blocks opioid receptors)
  • Partial agonist — Activates receptor with less-than-maximal response (e.g., buprenorphine)

Therapeutic Range & Drug Levels

Some drugs have a narrow therapeutic index (NTI) — the effective dose is dangerously close to the toxic dose. These require monitoring of serum drug levels:

DrugTherapeutic RangeToxicity Signs
Digoxin (Lanoxin)0.5–2.0 ng/mLAnorexia, N/V, yellow-green halos, bradycardia, dysrhythmias. Check K⁺ — hypokalemia ↑ toxicity risk.
Lithium (Lithobid)0.6–1.2 mEq/LCoarse tremor, confusion, seizures, oliguria. Maintain Na⁺ & hydration. Draw levels 12 hours after last dose.
Phenytoin (Dilantin)10–20 mcg/mLNystagmus, ataxia, gingival hyperplasia. Administer slowly IV — rate max 50 mg/min.
VancomycinTrough: 10–20 mcg/mLRed Man Syndrome (infuse over ≥ 60 min), nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity.
Aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin)Peak: 5–10 mcg/mL; Trough: < 2 mcg/mLNephrotoxicity, ototoxicity (tinnitus, hearing loss). Monitor creatinine & hearing.
Theophylline10–20 mcg/mLTachycardia, seizures, N/V. Avoid caffeine.
Valproic acid (Depakote)50–100 mcg/mLHepatotoxicity, pancreatitis, thrombocytopenia. Monitor LFTs & platelets.
Carbamazepine (Tegretol)4–12 mcg/mLAplastic anemia, agranulocytosis. Monitor CBC regularly.
🔴 When to HOLD a medication and notify the provider:
  • Drug level is above therapeutic range
  • Heart rate < 60 bpm before giving digoxin or beta-blockers
  • SBP < 100 mmHg before giving antihypertensives
  • RR < 12/min before giving opioids
  • K⁺ < 3.5 mEq/L before giving digoxin (hypokalemia ↑ dig toxicity)

🧮 Section 2 — Dosage Calculations & Administration

Essential Formulas

CalculationFormulaExample
Desired over HaveDose = (D / H) × VOrder: 500 mg. Available: 250 mg/5 mL → (500/250) × 5 = 10 mL
IV Flow Rate (mL/hr)mL/hr = Total Volume ÷ Hours1000 mL over 8 hr → 125 mL/hr
IV Drip Rate (gtt/min)(Volume × Drop Factor) ÷ (Time in min)1000 mL × 15 gtt/mL ÷ 480 min = 31 gtt/min
Weight-Based (mcg/kg/min)Rate = (Dose × Weight × 60) ÷ ConcentrationUsed for dopamine, dobutamine, nitroprusside
Pediatric (mg/kg)Dose = mg/kg × child's weight in kg15 mg/kg × 22 kg = 330 mg
BSA (m²)√ (height cm × weight kg ÷ 3600)Used for chemotherapy & pediatric dosing
⚡ NCLEX Rule: ALWAYS convert units first (g → mg, lb → kg). 1 kg = 2.2 lb. If the calculated dose seems unreasonably high or low, RE-CALCULATE before administering.

Common Drop Factors

  • Macrodrip: 10, 15, or 20 gtt/mL — used for standard IV infusions
  • Microdrip: 60 gtt/mL — used for pediatric and precise medication delivery; mL/hr = gtt/min
  • Blood tubing: 10 gtt/mL — large bore for blood products

Routes of Administration

RouteOnsetNursing Considerations
IVImmediateMost rapid onset. Monitor for infiltration, phlebitis. High-alert drugs require pump.
IM10–30 minZ-track for irritating meds (iron dextran). Vastus lateralis for infants. Max 3 mL per site (adults).
SubQ15–30 minInsulin, heparin, enoxaparin. Rotate sites. Do NOT aspirate for SubQ injections.
Oral30–60 minMost common route. Affected by first-pass metabolism. Hold if NPO (check with provider).
Sublingual1–3 minNitroglycerin SL — place under tongue, no swallowing until dissolved. May repeat × 3 at 5-min intervals.
TransdermalVariable (hours)Remove old patch before applying new. Rotate sites. MRI — remove any metallic-backed patches.
Rectal15–30 minUseful when patient is NPO or vomiting. Insert past internal sphincter.
InhalationMinutesBronchodilator (albuterol) FIRST, then corticosteroid. Rinse mouth after inhaled corticosteroids.
🟢 Injection Site Selection:
  • Deltoid: Max 1 mL. Vaccines, small-volume IM injections
  • Ventrogluteal: Preferred for IM in adults. Least risk of nerve injury
  • Vastus lateralis: Preferred for infants < 12 months
  • Dorsogluteal: AVOID — risk of sciatic nerve injury
⚠️ Section 3 — High-Alert Medications & Safety

Independent Double-Check Required

These high-alert medications require two nurses to independently verify before administration:

  • Insulin — all IV drips & concentrated formulations
  • Heparin — IV drips and high-dose SubQ
  • Blood products — verify at bedside with 2 RNs using 2 patient identifiers
  • Chemotherapy — complex protocols with narrow margins of safety
  • Opioid PCA pumps — programming verification
  • Potassium chloride (KCl) IV — NEVER give IV push; always diluted, always on a pump
  • Pediatric weight-based calculations — second nurse verifies math
🔴 NEVER Do These:
  • NEVER give KCl IV push — causes fatal cardiac arrest
  • NEVER crush enteric-coated or sustained-release medications
  • NEVER give methotrexate daily (weekly dosing for RA — daily = fatal overdose)
  • NEVER use trailing zeros (1.0 mg → write "1 mg") or skip leading zeros (0.5 mg, not .5 mg)
  • NEVER abbreviate: U (units), IU, QD, QOD, MS, MgSO₄ — use full words

Allergic & Anaphylactic Reactions

SeveritySignsNursing Action
Mild allergicRash, urticaria, pruritusStop drug. Notify provider. Administer diphenhydramine (Benadryl) as ordered.
AnaphylaxisThroat tightening, stridor, hypotension, tachycardia, wheezingSTOP drug immediately. Epinephrine 0.3 mg IM (anterolateral thigh). Maintain airway. Call rapid response.
Serum sicknessFever, joint pain, rash — occurs 7–14 days after exposureDelayed reaction. Notify provider. Treat symptoms.

Blood Product Administration

  • Pre-transfusion: Verify with 2 RNs at bedside using 2 patient identifiers + blood bank band. Check ABO/Rh compatibility.
  • Vital signs: Before, at 15 min, every 30 min, and after completion
  • Rate: Start slowly (2 mL/min for first 15 min). Complete within 4 hours.
  • Only NS: Use 0.9% Normal Saline with blood products — NEVER lactated Ringer's, D5W, or medications in the same line
Transfusion Reaction — Act Immediately:
  • STOP the transfusion immediately
  • Disconnect blood tubing — infuse NS with NEW tubing
  • Maintain IV access — do NOT remove the line
  • Notify provider and blood bank
  • Send blood bag & tubing + patient blood sample to the lab
  • Signs: fever, chills, flank pain, dark urine, hypotension, tachycardia, dyspnea

Medication Error Prevention

  • Look-alike / Sound-alike: Celebrex vs Celexa; metformin vs metoprolol; hydroxyzine vs hydralazine
  • Tall Man Lettering: hydrOXYzine vs hydrALAZINE — emphasizes critical letters
  • ISMP Do Not Crush List: SR, XL, ER, EC formulations must be swallowed whole
  • Report all errors — no matter how minor. Use incident reporting system. Focus on system fixes, not blame.

Cardiac Glycosides & Antidysrhythmics

  • Digoxin (Lanoxin) — Check apical pulse for full minute before giving. Hold if HR < 60 bpm (adult) or < 100 bpm (infant). Monitor K⁺ (hypokalemia ↑ dig toxicity).
  • Amiodarone — Broad-spectrum antidysrhythmic. Pulmonary toxicity (monitor for cough/dyspnea), thyroid dysfunction, corneal deposits, photosensitivity. Requires baseline PFTs & TFTs.
  • Adenosine — Rapid IV push followed immediately by NS flush. Used for SVT. Patient may feel brief chest tightness or transient asystole (expected).
  • Atropine — First-line for symptomatic bradycardia. Contraindicated in hypothermic patients.

Nitrates & Antianginals

Nitroglycerin (NTG) Protocol:
  • SL: 1 tablet q5min × 3 doses max. If pain persists after 1st dose → call 911 / activate EMS.
  • Check BP before each dose — hold if SBP < 90 mmHg
  • Headache is expected (vasodilation) — treat with acetaminophen
  • NEVER give with sildenafil (Viagra) or other PDE5 inhibitors — fatal hypotension
  • Store in dark glass container. Replace every 6 months. Should tingle under tongue if potent.

Statins & Lipid-Lowering Agents

  • Statins ("-statin": atorvastatin, simvastatin, rosuvastatin) — Take at bedtime (cholesterol synthesis peaks at night). Monitor LFTs. Report muscle pain (rhabdomyolysis risk → check CK levels). Avoid grapefruit juice.
  • Cholestyramine (Questran) — Bile acid sequestrant. Mix with fluid, never dry. Take other meds 1 hr before or 4 hr after (interferes with absorption).

Draw Regular (clear) FIRST → then draw NPH (cloudy). Think: RN = Regular before NPH.

Oral Hypoglycemics

  • Metformin (Glucophage) — First-line for Type 2 DM. Hold 48 hr before and after contrast dye (risk of lactic acidosis). Contraindicated in renal impairment (GFR < 30). GI side effects common. Does NOT cause hypoglycemia alone.
  • Sulfonylureas (glipizide, glyburide) — Stimulate insulin secretion. CAN cause hypoglycemia. Take 30 min before meals.
  • SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) — Cause glycosuria. Risk of UTI, genital yeast infections. Monitor for euglycemic DKA.

Thyroid Medications

  • Levothyroxine (Synthroid) — Hypothyroidism replacement. Take on empty stomach, 30–60 min before breakfast. Separate from calcium, iron, antacids by 4 hours. Monitor TSH (should decrease with treatment).
  • Methimazole (Tapazole) — Hyperthyroidism (blocks thyroid hormone synthesis). Monitor for agranulocytosis — report sore throat or fever immediately. TERATOGENIC in first trimester (use PTU instead).
  • PTU (Propylthiouracil) — Hyperthyroidism. Preferred in first-trimester pregnancy. Risk of hepatotoxicity. Monitor LFTs.

Corticosteroids

Prednisone / Methylprednisolone / Dexamethasone — Key Rules:
  • NEVER stop abruptly — taper gradually (risk of adrenal crisis)
  • Side effects: hyperglycemia, immunosuppression, osteoporosis, cushingoid features, GI bleeding
  • Take with food. Monitor blood glucose and K⁺
  • Avoid live vaccines during therapy
  • Report signs of infection (fever may be masked)
🧠 Section 6 — Neurological & Psychotropic Pharmacology

Antiepileptics / Anticonvulsants

DrugTherapeutic LevelCritical Nursing Points
Phenytoin (Dilantin)10–20 mcg/mLIV: compatible only with NS (precipitates in D5W). Max rate 50 mg/min. Side effects: gingival hyperplasia (oral care essential), hirsutism, nystagmus, ataxia. TERATOGENIC (fetal hydantoin syndrome).
Valproic acid (Depakote)50–100 mcg/mLMonitor LFTs, platelets, ammonia. Risk of hepatotoxicity & pancreatitis. TERATOGENIC. Do NOT give to children < 2 years with other anticonvulsants.
Carbamazepine (Tegretol)4–12 mcg/mLMonitor CBC — risk of aplastic anemia, agranulocytosis. Report sore throat, bruising, or fever. Induces CYP450 enzymes.
Levetiracetam (Keppra)No routine levelsWell-tolerated. Monitor for behavioral changes, mood alterations, somnolence. Dose adjust for renal impairment.
⚡ NCLEX Rule: NEVER abruptly stop anticonvulsants — risk of status epilepticus. Always taper. If a dose is missed, take it ASAP unless close to next dose.

Parkinson's Disease Medications

  • Levodopa/Carbidopa (Sinemet) — First-line. Restores dopamine. "On-off phenomenon" with long-term use. Avoid high-protein meals (↓ absorption). Urine may turn dark. Monitor for dyskinesias.
  • Dopamine agonists (pramipexole, ropinirole) — Risk of impulse control disorders (gambling, compulsive shopping), excessive sleepiness.
  • Anticholinergics (benztropine, trihexyphenidyl) — Reduce tremors. Side effects: dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, blurred vision. Contraindicated in glaucoma.

Antidepressants

ClassExamplesKey Points
SSRIsfluoxetine, sertraline, escitalopramFirst-line for depression & anxiety. BLACK BOX: ↑ suicidality in < 25 y/o. Takes 2–4 weeks for full effect. Serotonin syndrome risk if combined with MAOIs.
SNRIsvenlafaxine, duloxetineAlso treats neuropathic pain & fibromyalgia. Monitor BP (can ↑). Discontinuation syndrome if stopped abruptly.
TCAsamitriptyline, nortriptylineAnticholinergic side effects. Lethal in overdose (cardiac toxicity → widened QRS). Keep out of reach of suicidal patients.
MAOIsphenelzine, tranylcypromineTYRAMINE restriction (aged cheese, wine, cured meats → hypertensive crisis). Wait 14 days before switching to/from other antidepressants.
🔴 Serotonin Syndrome — Life-Threatening Emergency:
  • Cause: Excess serotonin — combining SSRIs + MAOIs, or SSRIs + tramadol, meperidine, triptans, linezolid
  • Signs: Hyperthermia, agitation, myoclonus (muscle jerking), diaphoresis, hyperreflexia, diarrhea, tachycardia
  • Treatment: Stop all serotonergic drugs. Cooling measures. Cyproheptadine (serotonin antagonist). Supportive care.

Antipsychotics

GenerationExamplesKey Side Effects
Typical (1st gen)haloperidol (Haldol), chlorpromazineEPS: dystonia, akathisia, tardive dyskinesia. Treat acute EPS with benztropine or diphenhydramine. Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS) risk.
Atypical (2nd gen)olanzapine (Zyprexa), quetiapine, risperidone, clozapineMetabolic syndrome: weight gain, hyperglycemia, dyslipidemia. Clozapine: requires ANC monitoring (risk of agranulocytosis — fatal without monitoring).
🔴 Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS):
  • Cause: Antipsychotic medications (especially high-potency typicals)
  • Signs: Hyperthermia (> 104°F), "lead-pipe" rigidity, altered mental status, autonomic instability, ↑ CK
  • Treatment: STOP antipsychotic immediately. Dantrolene + bromocriptine. Cooling measures. IV hydration. ICU admission.

Anxiolytics & Sedatives

  • Benzodiazepines (lorazepam, diazepam, midazolam, alprazolam) — CNS depression. Respiratory depression risk. Antidote: flumazenil. Avoid alcohol. Fall risk in elderly. Taper to discontinue (seizure risk).
  • Buspirone (BuSpar) — Non-sedating anxiolytic. Takes 2–4 weeks for effect. No abuse potential. Cannot be used PRN for acute anxiety. Avoid grapefruit juice.

Lithium — Bipolar Disorder

Lithium — Critical Monitoring:
  • Therapeutic range: 0.6–1.2 mEq/L. Draw levels 12 hr after last dose.
  • Narrow therapeutic index — toxicity begins at > 1.5 mEq/L
  • Maintain adequate Na⁺ and fluid intake — dehydration/low Na⁺ → ↑ lithium levels
  • Avoid NSAIDs, thiazides, ACE inhibitors (↑ lithium levels)
  • Early toxicity: fine tremors, N/V, diarrhea, drowsiness
  • Severe toxicity: coarse tremors, confusion, seizures, oliguria, coma
  • Monitor thyroid & renal function regularly (causes hypothyroidism & nephrogenic DI)

Antituberculars

🫁 RIPE — First-Line TB Treatment
Rifampin (orange body fluids, ↓ oral contraceptive effectiveness) · Isoniazid (INH) (hepatotoxic, give pyridoxine/B6 to prevent neuropathy) · Pyrazinamide (hepatotoxic, hyperuricemia) · Ethanbutol (visual changes — check acuity before starting)

Antivirals & Antifungals

  • Acyclovir (Zovirax) — Herpes, varicella. Maintain hydration (nephrotoxicity). IV: infuse slowly over ≥ 1 hour.
  • Oseltamivir (Tamiflu) — Influenza. Start within 48 hours of symptom onset for best effect.
  • Amphotericin B — Potent antifungal. Highly nephrotoxic ("Amphoterrible"). Premedicate with acetaminophen and diphenhydramine for infusion reactions. Monitor BUN/creatinine, K⁺, Mg²⁺.
  • Fluconazole (Diflucan) — Oral/systemic fungal infections. Monitor LFTs. Fewer side effects than amphotericin B.

Pain Management — Opioid Analgesics

DrugKey Points
MorphineGold standard for severe pain. Causes respiratory depression, constipation, hypotension, urinary retention. Hold if RR < 12/min. Antidote: naloxone (Narcan). Histamine release → pruritus.
Hydromorphone (Dilaudid)5–7× more potent than morphine. Used when morphine is not tolerated. Same precautions as morphine.
Fentanyl50–100× more potent than morphine. Transdermal patch: takes 12–24 hr for full effect. NEVER cut patches. Used for chronic pain.
Meperidine (Demerol)AVOID in elderly and renal impairment — metabolite (normeperidine) causes seizures. Avoid with MAOIs (serotonin syndrome). Not recommended for chronic pain.
CodeineMild-moderate pain. Some patients are ultra-rapid metabolizers (CYP2D6) → excessive response. AVOID in children < 12 y/o for cough.
🔴 Opioid Toxicity / Overdose:
  • Classic Triad: Respiratory depression (RR < 8) + pinpoint pupils + decreased LOC
  • Antidote: Naloxone (Narcan) 0.4–2 mg IV — may repeat q2-3min. Short duration — monitor for re-sedation.
  • Priority: Maintain airway + ventilation. Naloxone reverses respiratory depression within 1–2 minutes.

Non-Opioid Analgesics

  • Acetaminophen (Tylenol) — Max 3,000 mg/day (some sources: 4,000 mg/day for healthy adults; 2,000 mg/day with hepatic disease). Overdose → hepatic necrosis. Antidote: N-acetylcysteine (Mucomyst). No anti-inflammatory effect.
  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, ketorolac) — Anti-inflammatory, analgesic, antipyretic. Risk of GI bleeding, renal impairment, ↑ BP. Contraindicated in third trimester pregnancy. Ketorolac (Toradol): max 5 days use. Take with food.
⚡ NCLEX Pain Principle: Always assess pain BEFORE medicating and re-assess AFTER. Use the WHO Pain Ladder: non-opioid → weak opioid → strong opioid. Multimodal analgesia is preferred.

Common NCLEX Pharmacology Traps

  • Trap: "Give the medication and observe" — If vitals are outside hold parameters, the answer is HOLD and notify the provider, not give and observe.
  • Trap: A medication side effect is presented as a new symptom — recognize common drug side effects (e.g., ACE cough, statin myalgia, diuretic hypokalemia).
  • Trap: "Administer the PRN antianxiety medication" for a pre-op patient — always assess first. Anxiety is normal pre-op. Non-pharmacological interventions first.
  • Trap: Mixing up antidotes — memorize the antidote pairs. If unsure, the safest action is usually to stop the drug and call the provider.
  • Trap: Giving medications past hold parameters — never give digoxin if HR < 60, never give antihypertensives if SBP < 100. Even if it's "time for the medication."
